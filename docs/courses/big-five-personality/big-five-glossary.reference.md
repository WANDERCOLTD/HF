# Big Five Personality — Glossary

> **Document type:** REFERENCE · **Audience:** learner · **Default category:** vocabulary
>
> A flat term glossary for the Big Five (OCEAN) course. The five traits, fifteen BFI-2 facets (Soto & John 2017), and eight key concepts. Trait and facet names are descriptive labels documented in the published literature; specific instrument items are licensed and not reproduced here.

---

## The Five Traits

**Openness (Open-Mindedness)** — Engagement with novelty, abstraction, art, and complexity. People high in Openness are curious, imaginative, and drawn to new ideas; people low in Openness prefer familiar, concrete, and routine experiences. Sometimes called Open-Mindedness in the BFI-2; both names refer to the same construct.

**Conscientiousness** — Goal-directed behaviour, self-discipline, and organisation. High-Conscientiousness people are reliable, planful, and finish what they start. Low-Conscientiousness people are more spontaneous, flexible, and tolerant of disorder.

**Extraversion** — Social engagement, assertiveness, and energy. High-Extraversion people draw energy from social interaction and tend to be active in pursuing goals; low-Extraversion people are quieter and prefer fewer, deeper interactions.

**Agreeableness** — Concern for others, prosocial values, and cooperation. High-Agreeableness people prioritise harmony and others' welfare; low-Agreeableness people prioritise their own positions and are more comfortable with conflict.

**Negative Emotionality** — Tendency to experience negative emotions (anxiety, depression, frustration) under stress. The Soto & John (2017) BFI-2 rename of what was previously called Neuroticism. The rename was deliberate: the older term was clinically loaded and morally judgmental in colloquial use.

## The Fifteen Facets (BFI-2)

### Open-Mindedness facets

**Intellectual Curiosity** — Interest in ideas, abstract thinking, and analytical reasoning. The "I like figuring out how things work" facet of Openness.

**Aesthetic Sensitivity** — Responsiveness to art, beauty, music, and emotional/sensory experiences. Distinct from intellectual interest — a person can be high on one facet and low on the other.

**Creative Imagination** — Capacity for generating novel ideas, imagining counterfactuals, and producing creative output. The "what if it were different" facet of Openness.

### Conscientiousness facets

**Organization** — The tendency to keep order, tidiness, and structure in one's environment. "A place for everything."

**Productiveness** — The tendency to follow through on tasks, sustain effort, and finish what one starts. Distinct from Organization — a person can be highly productive in an apparently messy environment, or impeccably organised but procrastinate.

**Responsibility** — Reliability, dependability, and meeting one's commitments to others.

### Extraversion facets

**Sociability** — Enjoyment of social contact and the company of others. The "outgoing" facet — the one most people mean when they say "extravert".

**Assertiveness** — Willingness to express one's opinions, take leadership, and influence others. Independent of Sociability — a person can be high-Assertiveness and low-Sociability ("quiet leader") or vice versa.

**Energy Level** — General activity level, pace of life, and intensity of engagement. Sometimes called Activity. The third pillar of Extraversion.

### Agreeableness facets

**Compassion** — Empathic concern for others' welfare, especially those in distress. The warmth dimension of Agreeableness.

**Respectfulness** — Politeness, consideration of others, and avoidance of giving offense. Independent of underlying warmth — a person can be respectful without being warm, or warm but blunt.

**Trust** — Default assumption that others have good intentions. The most context-dependent facet of Agreeableness — trust levels can vary dramatically between domains (high trust with close friends, low trust in professional settings, for example).

### Negative Emotionality facets

**Anxiety** — Trait-level baseline worry about future events. Distinct from clinical anxiety disorder, which is diagnosed by frequency, severity, and functional impairment.

**Depression** — Trait-level tendency toward low mood, sadness, and pessimism. Like Anxiety, distinct from clinical depression as a diagnosed condition.

**Emotional Volatility** — Amplitude of emotional response to events. The "how much do moods shift" facet — independent of baseline trait-Anxiety. A person can have high trait-Anxiety but stable mood, or low baseline anxiety but volatile reactions to specific triggers.

## Key Concepts

**Five-Factor Model (FFM)** — The full name of the Big Five framework in academic literature. Used interchangeably with "the Big Five" and "the OCEAN model."

**OCEAN** — A mnemonic for the five trait initials: **O**penness, **C**onscientiousness, **E**xtraversion, **A**greeableness, **N**egative Emotionality (formerly Neuroticism). Some authors prefer CANOE; the order is interchangeable.

**Lexical Hypothesis** — The research strategy that produced the Big Five. The premise: socially important personality differences will be encoded in language. Therefore the structure of personality-describing vocabulary should reveal the structure of personality. Originated with Allport & Odbert (1936); operationalised by Cattell, Tupes & Christal, Norman, and Goldberg over the following decades.

**Dimensional vs Categorical** — The Big Five is *dimensional* — each trait is a continuous score, not a yes/no category. This is the key distinction from MBTI and similar typologies, which compress continuous traits into binary categories ("Introvert" vs "Extravert"). The Big Five's dimensional structure preserves more information.

**Facet** — A sub-trait within a domain (e.g. Sociability is a facet of Extraversion). The BFI-2 measures three facets per trait; the NEO-PI-R measures six. Facets correlate within their parent trait but are not interchangeable — a high overall trait score can mask wide variation across facets.

**Trait Anxiety vs State Anxiety** — Trait Anxiety is a stable personality baseline (a facet of Negative Emotionality). State Anxiety is a temporary response to a specific situation (e.g. before a job interview). A person can be high trait-Anxiety but low state-Anxiety in a given moment, or low trait-Anxiety but acutely anxious in a specific situation.

**Cognitive vs Affective Empathy** — Two distinct dimensions of empathy. *Cognitive* empathy is the ability to understand what others feel intellectually. *Affective* empathy is the capacity to share or feel what others feel. These can dissociate — a person can have intact cognitive empathy with diminished affective empathy (a profile sometimes seen in narcissistic personality patterns). Not strictly a Big Five concept, but useful contrast vocabulary for the Compassion facet of Agreeableness.

**MBTI / 16Personalities** — The Myers-Briggs Type Indicator and its derivatives (16Personalities, KTS, etc.). The most popular alternative to the Big Five in non-academic contexts. The MBTI compresses continuous trait scores into 16 binary "types" via four binary dichotomies (E/I, N/S, T/F, J/P). The Big Five literature consistently finds (a) the MBTI's binary scoring loses information present in the continuous trait scales, (b) the MBTI's reliability over repeated administration is markedly lower than the Big Five's, and (c) the MBTI's four dimensions overlap imperfectly with four of the five Big Five traits, with no MBTI dimension corresponding to Negative Emotionality at all (Pittenger 1993; McCrae & Costa 1989).
