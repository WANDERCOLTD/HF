## Trait Theorists

Trait theorists believe personality can be understood via the approach that all people have certain traits, or characteristic ways of behaving. Do you tend to be sociable or shy? Passive or aggressive? Optimistic or pessimistic? Moody or even-tempered? Early trait theorists tried to describe all human personality traits. For example, one trait theorist, Gordon Allport (Allport & Odbert, 1936), found 4,500 words in the English language that could describe people. He organized these personality traits into three categories: cardinal traits, central traits, and secondary traits. A cardinal trait is one that dominates your entire personality, and hence your life — such as Ebenezer Scrooge's greed and Mother Teresa's altruism. Cardinal traits are not very common: Few people have personalities that consist of a single trait. Instead, our personalities typically are composed of multiple traits. Central traits are those that make up our personalities (such as loyal, kind, agreeable, friendly, sneaky, wild, and grouchy). Secondary traits are those that are not quite as obvious or as consistent as central traits. They are present under specific circumstances and include preferences and attitudes. For example, one person gets angry when people try to tickle him; another can only sleep on the left side of the bed; and yet another always orders her salad dressing on the side. And you — although not normally an anxious person — feel nervous before making a speech in front of your English class.

In an effort to make the list of traits more manageable, Raymond Cattell (1946, 1957) narrowed down the list to 16 factors and developed a personality assessment, called the 16PF. Later, psychologists Hans and Sybil Eysenck focused on temperament (Eysenck, 1990, 1992; Eysenck, Eysenck & Barrett, 1985) and hypothesized that two major dimensions of personality captured the essence of temperament: extroversion/introversion and neuroticism/stability. Extroversion/introversion captures the degree to which people are outgoing and social vs. quiet, reserved, and self-focused. Neuroticism/stability reflects how emotionally reactive a person is — anxious, moody, sad people are high in neuroticism; people who are emotionally stable do not experience these emotional ups and downs as intensely. The Eysencks proposed that combinations of high or low levels of these two dimensions produced four basic personality types: melancholic (high neuroticism, introverted), choleric (high neuroticism, extroverted), phlegmatic (low neuroticism, introverted), and sanguine (low neuroticism, extroverted). Later, the Eysencks added a third dimension: psychoticism vs. superego control (Eysenck, Eysenck & Barrett, 1985).

## The Five Factor Model

The most popular theory in personality psychology today is the Five Factor Model. Five factors (often called the Big Five) are dimensions of personality used to describe human personality (Costa & McCrae, 1987; Digman, 1990; Goldberg, 1993). The Five Factors are openness, conscientiousness, extroversion, agreeableness, and neuroticism. A helpful way to remember the Five Factors is by using the mnemonic OCEAN. In the Five Factor Model, each person has each factor, but they occur along a spectrum.

### Openness

Openness is characterized by imagination, feelings, actions, and ideas. People who score high on this factor tend to be curious and have a wide range of interests.

### Conscientiousness

Conscientiousness is characterized by competence, self-discipline, thoughtfulness, and achievement-striving (goal-directed behavior). People who score high on this factor are hardworking and dependable. Numerous studies have found a positive correlation between conscientiousness and academic success (Akomolafe, 2013; Chamorro-Premuzic & Furnham, 2003; Conrad & Patry, 2012; Noftle & Robins, 2007; Wagerman & Funder, 2007).

### Extroversion

Extroversion is characterized by sociability, assertiveness, excitement-seeking, and emotional expression. People who score high on this factor are usually described as outgoing and warm. Not surprisingly, people who score high on both extroversion and openness are more likely to participate in adventure and risky sports due to their curious and excitement-seeking nature (Tok, 2011).

### Agreeableness

Agreeableness is characterized by trust, altruism, kindness, and affection. Highly agreeable people tend to have high prosocial behaviors. They are more likely to be volunteering and to express altruism. Children with high agreeableness are more likely to be regarded by both peers and teachers as being likeable, kind, considerate, and able to handle frustration well (Jensen-Campbell et al., 2002).

### Neuroticism

Neuroticism is the tendency to experience negative emotions. People high on neuroticism tend to experience emotional instability and are characterized as angry, impulsive, and hostile. Watson and Clark (1984) found that people reporting high levels of neuroticism also tend to report feeling anxious and unhappy. In contrast, people who score low in neuroticism tend to be calm and even-tempered.

## How Traits Develop

Personality traits are stable over time, but they can change. The traits of openness and neuroticism tend to decline slightly with age, whereas agreeableness tends to increase (Soto, John, Gosling, & Potter, 2011). It is unclear whether these changes reflect biological maturation, social learning, or simply different self-perceptions across the lifespan.

While the Five Factors have been replicated across cultures, including non-Western contexts (McCrae & Costa, 1997), the relative emphasis on each factor and the precise behaviors that load onto each factor can vary. For example, the Openness factor shows more variation in expression across cultures than the other four. Cross-cultural personality researchers are generally cautious about claiming the Big Five is "universal" in the strict sense — rather, the five dimensions emerge with reasonable consistency, but their content and expression are culturally inflected.

## Critiques of Trait Theory

Trait theorists have been criticized for failing to consider the role of situational forces in shaping behavior. Many critics argue that traits, while useful for description, do not adequately explain the variability of behavior across different situations. Walter Mischel (1968) was particularly influential in raising this critique: he argued that the correlation between trait measures and actual behavior is typically modest (around 0.30), suggesting that traits leave most of the variance unexplained. Modern trait theorists respond that traits predict behavior averaged across many situations more reliably than behavior in any single situation — a position now supported by extensive longitudinal data.
