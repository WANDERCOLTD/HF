## Why Read Weltman Alongside Cialdini

Cialdini gives you the underlying mechanisms — the seven principles, the cognitive shortcuts they exploit, the cross-cultural validation. *Influence* reads like a researcher describing what he found.

Josh Weltman gives you the practitioner voice — the move-by-move craft of using those principles in actual advertising work. *Seducing Strangers* reads like a creative director walking you through how to write a pitch, what to lead with, what to cut. Weltman was a creative director at Cole & Weber for two decades and consulted on AMC's *Mad Men* — his framings come from inside the ad world rather than from academic research.

The two books complement each other. Cialdini tells you *why* something works; Weltman tells you *how to do it* and gives you the language for what good practitioners actually call the moves. This course uses Cialdini as the structural spine (the principles are what we test the learner on) and Weltman as the voice the tutor reaches for when explaining what a principle looks like in practice.

## Weltman's Frame — Attention, Attraction, Action

Weltman's overarching arc for any persuasion task is three moves in sequence: **attention, attraction, action**.

**Attention** is the threshold problem. Before any persuasion can occur, the audience has to be looking. In a world saturated with messages, getting attention is a real cost — not a free preliminary step. The persuader's job at this stage is to be unexpected enough to break through ambient noise, but not so unexpected that the audience disengages defensively. Weltman frames this as a tightrope: too familiar and the audience scrolls past; too strange and the audience pulls back.

**Attraction** is the seduction phase proper. Once the audience is looking, the persuader has to make the offer feel desirable rather than transactional. This is where the principles do most of their work — liking, social proof, and the storytelling moves Weltman emphasises throughout. Attraction is about lowering the audience's resistance to engagement, not pushing them harder.

**Action** is the close. After attention and attraction, the persuader makes the ask. The ask has to be matched to what the prior phases earned — too small an ask leaves opportunity on the table; too large breaks the connection. Most persuasion failures, in Weltman's diagnosis, are not failures at the action stage; they are failures at attention or attraction that the action stage cannot rescue.

The three-phase frame is not original to Weltman — it appears in various forms across copywriting and rhetoric traditions — but he applies it cleanly enough that it works as a teaching shorthand. This course uses the phrase "attention → attraction → action" as the dinner-party handle the learner will carry home.

## Make the Unfamiliar Familiar

One of Weltman's most useful moves: when introducing something new, make it feel familiar. The audience cannot evaluate a wholly novel proposition; they need an anchor. The persuader's job is to provide that anchor — by connecting the new thing to something the audience already understands and values.

This applies the **Liking** principle (Cialdini ch. 3) in a specific way: rather than engineering liking for the persuader, you engineer liking for the *offer* by tying it to existing affection. The startup that explains itself as "Airbnb for X" is using this move (lazily, but functionally). The luxury brand that frames a new product through its heritage is doing the same. The political campaign that lands a candidate by associating them with a previous beloved figure also.

The misuse pattern: false analogies that imply similarity to a beloved reference that doesn't actually hold. The "we're just like [Apple, Tesla, Disney]" pitch fails when the audience compares and finds the resemblance superficial. The move only works when the underlying connection is real enough to survive scrutiny.

## Stories Beat Bullet Points

Weltman returns repeatedly to the primacy of story over information. People do not act on data; they act on narrative. A bullet point of features may be technically accurate but produces no emotional response and therefore no action. A single concrete story of one customer's experience produces more action than statistics about ten thousand.

This connects to the Heath brothers' *Made to Stick* (2007), which formalised the SUCCESS heuristic — ideas that survive are **Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories**. The two practitioners are not contradicting each other; they are working the same craft from different angles. The Heath brothers give the dimensional checklist; Weltman gives the move-level coaching.

The practical implication: when you have data and a story, lead with the story and use the data to back it up. When you only have data, find or build a story that exemplifies what the data says. When you only have a story, that is often enough; resist the urge to "professionalise" it with statistics that dilute the emotional punch.

## The Four Questions

Weltman organises a lot of persuasion analysis around four questions any persuasion piece should answer in the audience's head: **Who? What? How? Why?**

- **Who** is talking, and why should I listen to them? (Activates Authority + Liking + Unity.)
- **What** are they actually offering? (The substantive content.)
- **How** does it work — how would my life change if I accepted? (The mechanism, lightly explained.)
- **Why** does it matter — why now, why me? (The hook into the audience's existing situation.)

The four questions are not a script; they are a diagnostic. When persuasion is failing, Weltman's move is to check which of the four questions the audience cannot answer from the material in front of them. A persuasive piece that answers Who and What but leaves How and Why ambiguous will get attention but not action. A piece that answers How and Why beautifully but botches Who will be ignored because the source isn't trusted.

This frame maps cleanly onto Cialdini's principles. *Who* questions are answered by Authority, Liking, and Unity moves. *What* and *How* questions are central-route content (ELM). *Why* questions are answered by Scarcity (why now), Social Proof (why others are doing it), and Reciprocity (why you owe a hearing).

## Weltman on Ethics — A Practitioner's Frame

Weltman is, by his own admission, an advertising professional — not a moral philosopher. But the book takes a clear stance: **the long game in persuasion is honesty**, because the short game eats the practitioner's reputation. Manipulative tactics work once; they damage the brand's ability to do legitimate persuasion thereafter.

His operational test, paraphrased: would you be comfortable explaining to the audience exactly what you were doing and why? The honest persuader can answer yes; the manipulator cannot. This is essentially Cialdini's truth-test in a more practitioner-flavoured framing — and this course uses both phrasings interchangeably.

Weltman is also honest about the industry's track record. Plenty of advertising work fails the honesty test. The book's posture is not that every working creative is an ethical hero; it is that the practitioners who build durable careers are disproportionately the ones who learned the discipline of honest persuasion early.

## The SUCCESS Framework (Heath & Heath, 2007) — How Ideas Stick

Chip Heath and Dan Heath's *Made to Stick* (2007) developed the SUCCESS heuristic for what makes ideas memorable enough to spread. The six dimensions:

- **Simple** — the core idea reduces to a single sentence the audience can repeat
- **Unexpected** — there is a surprise element that breaks the audience's default expectation
- **Concrete** — the idea is grounded in specific, sensory, easily-pictured detail
- **Credible** — the source is trusted, or the audience can verify the claim
- **Emotional** — the idea evokes feeling (curiosity, fear, hope, anger) rather than just delivering information
- **Stories** — the idea is carried by narrative rather than abstraction

The Heath brothers' framework is a quality checklist for any persuasion artefact. Most ideas that fail to land fail on Simple (the idea is buried in three other ideas) or Concrete (the idea is too abstract for the audience to picture). This course references SUCCESS as the application framework — when the learner is building their own persuasion attempt in the application module, the framework gives them a checklist for the artefact, while Cialdini's principles give them the underlying levers.

## How This Course Uses Weltman and Cialdini Together

| Layer | Source | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism — *why does persuasion work?* | Cialdini 2021 | The seven principles + the cognitive shortcuts |
| Process — *what do you actually do?* | Weltman 2015 | Attention → attraction → action; the four questions; make-the-unfamiliar-familiar |
| Application checklist — *did the artefact you built work?* | Heath & Heath 2007 | SUCCESS dimensions |
| Cognitive frame — *when do peripheral cues work?* | Petty & Cacioppo 1986 | ELM central/peripheral routes |
| Loss/scarcity asymmetry — *why does scarcity hit harder?* | Kahneman & Tversky 1979 | Loss aversion (~2× weighting) |
| Ethical posture — *where's the line?* | Cialdini 2021 + Weltman 2015 (aligned) | Truth-test: amplifying what is true vs manufacturing what is not |

The course's pedagogical move is to teach a Cialdini principle, then have the learner see it in a Weltman-style worked example, then check the result against the SUCCESS checklist, then ask the truth-test question. Each layer reinforces the others.
