# Seducing Strangers — Question Bank

> **Document type:** QUESTION_BANK · **Intended classification:** Socratic probes mapped to course outcomes · **Question type:** TUTOR_QUESTION · **Assessment use:** FORMATIVE · **Bloom:** mostly UNDERSTAND and APPLY
>
> **Source:** HFF-authored Socratic probes **in the spirit of** the Cialdini, Weltman, and Heath frameworks. **No licensed instrument items reproduced.** Each probe is original to HFF and designed for conversational tutor delivery, not as a psychometric measurement. Named examples from the literature (Petrified Forest, Tupperware, Hare Krishna) are referenced in probe context but not reproduced from book text.
>
> **Format:** Each item below has (a) the question the tutor asks, (b) model responses at Emerging / Developing / Secure tiers, and (c) at least one misuse-pattern trap with a coaching note. Items are clustered by Outcome (OUT-NN). The tutor uses these as ammunition for Socratic exchange — not verbatim scripts.
>
> **Coverage:** 48 items across 16 outcomes (3 per outcome).

## OUT-01 — Seven principles + Weltman frame

### Item 1.1
**Q:** Cialdini documents seven persuasion principles. What does Weltman's "attention → attraction → action" frame add to that list?

- **Emerging:** "They're the same thing." (Conflates Cialdini's mechanisms with Weltman's process.)
- **Developing:** "Weltman is about sequence, Cialdini is about levers."
- **Secure:** "Weltman gives the *process* — what you do, in what order. Cialdini gives the *mechanisms* — why each move works on the audience's cognition. You need both: the sequence tells you when to deploy a principle; the principle tells you why it lands."

**Misuse trap:** Treating Weltman's three phases as if they're principles themselves. Coaching: they're stages, not levers.

### Item 1.2
**Q:** Name the seven Cialdini principles. Don't worry about order.

- **Emerging:** 1–3 named, possibly conflating Liking with Social Proof.
- **Developing:** 5–6 named, missing Unity (the 2021 addition) or Commitment.
- **Secure:** All seven — Reciprocity, Commitment/Consistency, Social Proof, Liking, Authority, Scarcity, Unity. Bonus: knows Unity was added in the 2021 edition.

### Item 1.3
**Q:** Why does Cialdini bother grouping persuasion techniques into seven categories? Why not 20, or 3, or 50?

- **Emerging:** "It's just convenient."
- **Developing:** "Researchers found these seven kept showing up."
- **Secure:** "Cialdini spent three years inside compliance professions watching practitioners use techniques that worked. He noticed they clustered around seven underlying cognitive mechanisms. The categories aren't arbitrary — they're the natural joints of the underlying shortcuts the brain uses to make fast decisions. Twenty would over-fit; three would lose real distinctions."

## OUT-02 — ELM + peripheral-route caveat

### Item 2.1
**Q:** What's the difference between central-route and peripheral-route processing?

- **Emerging:** "Central is fast, peripheral is slow." (Wrong — backward.)
- **Developing:** "Central is when you think carefully, peripheral is when you use shortcuts."
- **Secure:** "Central is deliberate, evidence-weighing, slow. Peripheral is heuristic, cue-driven, fast. Petty & Cacioppo 1986. Cialdini's principles mostly operate on the peripheral route — they exploit the shortcuts the brain runs when it's not elaborating centrally."

### Item 2.2
**Q:** A B2B procurement team is running a deep evaluation of vendor proposals. Which Cialdini principles still work well on this audience, and which weaken?

- **Emerging:** "They all work the same."
- **Developing:** "Some work, some don't — Authority probably still works."
- **Secure:** "High-elaboration audience, so peripheral cues lose power. Authority still works *if* it's earned and substantive (real expertise relevant to the claim) — credential-theatre fails. Social Proof works if the proof is from peer organisations, not consumer-style testimonials. Liking and Scarcity weaken substantially. Reciprocity can still work but the move has to be a *real* contribution (genuine analysis, useful data), not a trinket."

### Item 2.3
**Q:** Why does Cialdini's principle of Liking work less well when the audience notices it's being used?

- **Emerging:** "It just does."
- **Developing:** "Because they distrust it."
- **Secure:** "Once the audience moves from peripheral processing ('I like this person') to central processing ('wait, they're trying to make me like them'), the principle stops operating as a heuristic and starts operating as evidence of manipulation. The peripheral-route trick is invisible-to-the-audience. Made visible, it backfires."

## OUT-03 — Truth-test for ethical persuasion

### Item 3.1
**Q:** What's the operational test this course uses to distinguish ethical persuasion from manipulation?

- **Emerging:** "Is it lying?" (Close but underspecified.)
- **Developing:** "Whether the audience would still agree if they knew what you were doing."
- **Secure:** "The truth-test: would the audience still be persuaded if they could see exactly what was being done and why? If yes, the persuasion is amplifying something true. If no, it's manufacturing something the audience would reject — that's manipulation. Cialdini and Weltman both arrive at this test from different angles."

### Item 3.2
**Q:** Someone tells you persuasion is morally neutral — "it's just a tool, what you do with it is up to you." Steel-man the position, then say what's wrong with it.

- **Emerging:** Agrees fully or rejects fully without nuance.
- **Developing:** "The mechanism is neutral but how you use it matters."
- **Secure:** "The steel-man: mechanism *is* neutral — a knife cuts whether you cook or stab. The flaw: 'just a tool' is the language people use to disclaim responsibility for choices they could make differently. The mechanism's neutrality doesn't excuse the choice to deploy it dishonestly. Calling persuasion 'neutral' is a way of pretending the practitioner has no agency in how they apply it."

### Item 3.3
**Q:** Your boss tells you to add a fake countdown timer to the checkout page. What's the response?

- **Emerging:** "Refuse."
- **Developing:** "Push back, explain it'll hurt brand trust."
- **Secure:** "Range of responses. Push back with the evidence: fake scarcity degrades credibility budget across the entire site, not just the checkout. Propose A/B testing with *real* urgency (genuine end-of-month deal, actual inventory cap) against the fake. Document the request. The course isn't telling you to refuse heroically — it's making sure you see the choice clearly rather than dismissing it as inevitable."

**Misuse trap:** "Everyone does it, so it doesn't matter." Coaching: the cumulative effect on the digital commerce trust ecosystem matters even if your individual choice is small.

## OUT-04 — Reciprocity recognition

### Item 4.1
**Q:** A B2B sales rep sends you a free 20-page research report on your industry, unsolicited, before any conversation. What's the Cialdini move?

- **Emerging:** "Just being helpful."
- **Developing:** "Reciprocity."
- **Secure:** "Reciprocity. They're investing real work in advance — a 20-page report is a costly signal. The rule of debt kicks in: you feel a pull to give them at least a meeting in return. Whether it's ethical depends on whether the report is genuinely useful or padded filler designed to manufacture obligation."

### Item 4.2
**Q:** Why does reciprocity work even when the "favour" is something you didn't ask for?

- **Emerging:** "Politeness."
- **Developing:** "Social pressure to repay."
- **Secure:** "Reciprocity is older than logical evaluation — it's wired into the cooperative substrate of human social behaviour. The pressure to repay isn't computed; it's felt as obligation, regardless of whether you wanted the original favour. That's why supermarket free samples and trade-show pens work — the gift creates debt independent of whether the recipient sees it as a gift."

### Item 4.3
**Q:** Can you think of a time in the last week when reciprocity was used on you ethically — by someone amplifying something true?

- **Emerging:** Cannot generate; or names a manipulation example.
- **Developing:** Names a case but doesn't analyse the truth claim.
- **Secure:** Names a case AND identifies the truth the persuasion was amplifying: someone helped first because they actually had value to offer, the help was genuinely useful, and the eventual ask was proportionate to the help.

## OUT-05 — Social Proof recognition

### Item 5.1
**Q:** "Used by 50,000 companies including Stripe and Notion" on a SaaS homepage. What principle, and when does it work?

- **Emerging:** "Bragging."
- **Developing:** "Social proof."
- **Secure:** "Social proof — descriptive. Works strongly when (a) the visitor sees themselves as similar to Stripe / Notion type companies, (b) the visitor is in peripheral processing (early stage of evaluation), and (c) the count is credible. Weakens for sophisticated buyers in central processing who'd want references they can actually call, not just logos."

### Item 5.2
**Q:** What's the Petrified Forest case, and what's its lesson?

- **Emerging:** Cannot recall.
- **Developing:** "Park sign that backfired."
- **Secure:** "Cialdini et al. 2006 — National Park sign saying 'many past visitors have removed wood from the park,' intended as a warning, actually *increased* theft. The sign was inadvertently providing descriptive social proof — telling the audience this is what people do. The injunctive variant ('please don't — it diminishes the park') was more effective. Lesson: descriptive vs injunctive norm collision can reverse the intended effect."

### Item 5.3
**Q:** Why is "10,000 customers can't be wrong" sometimes a weak persuasion move even though it's social proof?

- **Emerging:** "Old-fashioned."
- **Developing:** "Reads as commodity."
- **Secure:** "Several issues: (a) commodity-coded — premium brands don't shout volume; (b) the 'customers' may be perceived as dissimilar to the specific visitor; (c) high-elaboration audiences want references and case studies, not aggregate counts; (d) saturation — every site claims it, so the move has lost informational value. Better moves: specific named peer organisations, or volume framed as evidence of scale ('infrastructure trusted by half the Fortune 100') rather than as collective endorsement."

## OUT-06 — Reciprocity-misuse

### Item 6.1
**Q:** A trade show vendor presses a free pen into your hand, then asks for a 30-minute demo. Is this manipulative reciprocity?

- **Emerging:** "Yes — it's a trick."
- **Developing:** "Depends on the demo."
- **Secure:** "Lean toward manipulation, but it depends. The asymmetry test: was the 'gift' proportionate to the ask? A pen for a 30-minute demo is wildly asymmetric — the pen was cover, not contribution. Compare: a free in-depth research report for a 30-minute meeting is more balanced. The principle is real; manipulative reciprocity is when the gift is just there to manufacture debt rather than provide value."

### Item 6.2
**Q:** Cold outreach that opens with "I gave you a follow on LinkedIn, so could you take 15 minutes for a call?" — what's happening?

- **Emerging:** "Just an ask."
- **Developing:** "Trying to use reciprocity."
- **Secure:** "Manipulative reciprocity — the 'favour' is trivial and costless to the asker (a LinkedIn follow takes 2 seconds), but it's invoked to create a sense of debt for a substantially larger ask (15 minutes). The asymmetry is the tell. Honest reciprocity matches investment to ask; manipulative reciprocity inflates trivial gestures into leverage."

### Item 6.3
**Q:** How do you tell the difference between someone being genuinely generous and someone deploying manipulative reciprocity?

- **Emerging:** "You can't always."
- **Developing:** "Watch for what they ask for next."
- **Secure:** "Two tests: (a) proportionality — does the size of the original gift roughly match what they later ask for, or is it dramatically smaller? (b) framing of refusals — when you decline, do they accept gracefully or do they invoke the favour ('but I helped you with X')? Manipulative reciprocity fails both tests; genuine generosity passes both. The principle itself is neutral — the difference is whether the giver believes they've earned what they're asking for."

## OUT-07 — Social-Proof-misuse

### Item 7.1
**Q:** A SaaS startup's homepage shows 12 customer logos including major Fortune 500 brands. Three of those logos turn out to be customers' subsidiaries with $50/month accounts. Manipulation?

- **Emerging:** "No — they were customers."
- **Developing:** "Technically true but misleading."
- **Secure:** "Yes, manipulation. The truth-test: would a visitor still be persuaded if they could see that those Fortune 500 logos represent a $50/month account at an obscure subsidiary? Almost certainly not — they'd feel misled. The logos *amplify* a literal truth ('we have these customers') but *manufacture* an implied truth ('we have significant relationships with these enterprises'). The implied truth is what does the persuasion; manufacturing it is manipulation."

### Item 7.2
**Q:** A direct-to-consumer brand has 4.7-star reviews on Trustpilot. Investigators find that ~30% of reviews were left within 24 hours of order placement, before the customer could have used the product. Is the 4.7 honest?

- **Emerging:** "If they're real customers, yes."
- **Developing:** "Probably gamed."
- **Secure:** "No — even if every reviewer is a real customer, reviewing a product before using it isn't an honest evaluation. The 4.7 manufactures a confidence claim ('4.7 stars from real users who used it') from a different reality ('4.7 stars from real users at checkout euphoria'). Standard manipulation pattern: incentivise immediate reviews via discount codes, then aggregate them as quality signal."

### Item 7.3
**Q:** Why does manufactured social proof damage *legitimate* social proof in the same ecosystem?

- **Emerging:** "It doesn't."
- **Developing:** "People stop trusting reviews."
- **Secure:** "Audiences who get burned by fake reviews learn to distrust *all* aggregate review signals — including the legitimate ones. The credibility budget for social-proof claims is shared across the ecosystem; every manipulation reduces the available trust for honest practitioners. This is why legitimate brands now go to lengths to demonstrate verification (Verified Purchase, photo evidence, vendor-side moderation logs). The misuse of the principle imposes a tax on everyone."

## OUT-08 — Scarcity + Prospect Theory

### Item 8.1
**Q:** Why does "only 3 left at this price" pull harder than "70% off"?

- **Emerging:** "More urgent."
- **Developing:** "It's about losing something."
- **Secure:** "Scarcity activates loss aversion (Kahneman & Tversky 1979 — losses ~2× as painful as equivalent gains feel good). '70% off' is a gain frame; 'only 3 left' is a loss frame ('you might miss this'). Same economic content, asymmetric pull because of the underlying weighting."

### Item 8.2
**Q:** Loss aversion is "approximately 2:1." What does that mean operationally?

- **Emerging:** "Losses are bigger."
- **Developing:** "Losing $100 feels twice as bad as gaining $100 feels good."
- **Secure:** "Right. The implication for persuasion: framing the same offer as a loss recruits roughly twice the motivational weight as framing it as a gain. The ratio varies by domain and stake size — bigger in some contexts, smaller in others — but the asymmetry is robust. This is why 'don't miss this' tends to outperform 'gain this' in equivalent copy."

### Item 8.3
**Q:** A wholesaler experiment found that pitching a tightening supply *plus* the fact that this information wasn't widely known produced six times the orders of the standard pitch. What's happening?

- **Emerging:** "Scarcity worked."
- **Developing:** "Scarcity plus exclusivity."
- **Secure:** "Two principles stacked. Scarcity (the supply is tightening) + Exclusivity (you know something others don't). The exclusivity flips the frame from 'one of many buyers competing' to 'insider with an information advantage,' which is its own form of value. The 6× lift shows that stacked-principle deployments can multiply rather than just add. Cialdini's case study."

## OUT-09 — Scarcity-misuse

### Item 9.1
**Q:** A site has a "limited offer — 24 hours left!" countdown timer. You close the tab and re-open it the next day. The timer still says 24 hours. What's happening, and why does it matter?

- **Emerging:** "Timer's broken."
- **Developing:** "Fake scarcity."
- **Secure:** "Manufactured scarcity — the timer is decorative, not real. Why it matters: every time an audience detects fake scarcity, the credibility budget for *all* the site's claims drops. The next time the site says 'limited' or 'exclusive,' the audience discounts it. The site has traded short-term lift for long-term credibility loss — and the loss compounds across the digital commerce ecosystem because audiences learn the pattern and apply it elsewhere."

### Item 9.2
**Q:** Why is Cialdini's Scarcity principle the most credibility-fragile of the seven?

- **Emerging:** Cannot answer.
- **Developing:** "People can check if scarcity is real."
- **Secure:** "Scarcity is testable in real-time — close the tab, come back, see if it changed. The other principles are harder to falsify in the moment (was the reciprocity gift sincere? Is the authority real?). Scarcity puts the falsification test right in front of the audience. Once the test fails, the entire principle is suspect across all future deployments by that brand."

### Item 9.3
**Q:** An ecommerce brand wants more urgency in their email. Real urgency-creating moves vs the manipulative ones?

- **Emerging:** Conflates the two.
- **Developing:** "Real moves work the same but aren't fake."
- **Secure:** "Real moves: actual cap on inventory (genuinely 12 left), genuine end-of-promotion dates (the sale ends because the marketing calendar says so, and won't restart next week), legitimate seasonal scarcity (Christmas tree the week before Christmas). Manipulative moves: countdown timers that reset, 'limited' offers that are evergreen, fake low-stock displays. The audience-side difference: after the purchase, would the buyer still feel the urgency was real, or would they feel manipulated?"

## OUT-10 — Loss vs gain framing

### Item 10.1
**Q:** A health insurance pitch can frame the same offer as "save $200/year" or "you're losing $200/year by not having this plan." Which works better, and on whom?

- **Emerging:** "The first one is friendlier."
- **Developing:** "Loss frame is stronger because of loss aversion."
- **Secure:** "Loss frame is generally stronger because of the 2:1 asymmetry. BUT — it's not universal. Loss frames can also trigger defensive disengagement ('feels manipulative, ignore'). Sophisticated audiences resist them. Best practice: loss frame for audiences in early-stage low-elaboration evaluation; gain frame for audiences who are already engaged and want to feel positive momentum."

### Item 10.2
**Q:** When should you NOT use a loss frame, even though it pulls harder?

- **Emerging:** "Always use loss — it works better."
- **Developing:** "When it feels manipulative."
- **Secure:** "Several cases: (a) audience in central-route processing — they'll detect the asymmetric framing and discount it; (b) brand context where loss-framing conflicts with brand values (wellness, generosity, abundance); (c) audiences already at high-anxiety baseline where additional loss salience produces avoidance, not action; (d) sophisticated B2B where the loss frame reads as desperate sales tactic. The asymmetry is real; the application has limits."

### Item 10.3
**Q:** What's the difference between scarcity and loss framing — they sound similar?

- **Emerging:** "Same thing."
- **Developing:** "Scarcity is about the thing, loss is about the buyer."
- **Secure:** "Scarcity is *about the item* — it might run out, supply is tightening. Loss framing is *about the buyer's outcome* — they might lose access, status, opportunity. They lean on the same underlying loss-aversion asymmetry but the frame differs. Scarcity says 'the item might disappear'; loss framing says 'you might miss out.' Often used together, but operationally distinct."

## OUT-11 — Liking + make-the-unfamiliar-familiar

### Item 11.1
**Q:** A startup pitches itself as "Airbnb for X." What principle, and what's the move?

- **Emerging:** "Comparison."
- **Developing:** "Making something new feel familiar."
- **Secure:** "Liking — applied to the offer rather than the persuader. Weltman's 'make the unfamiliar familiar.' The audience can't evaluate a wholly novel proposition; anchoring it to Airbnb gives them a fast frame ('I know how Airbnb works; this is like that'). The move works when the analogy is structurally honest. It fails when the resemblance is superficial — 'Airbnb for X' where X is enterprise software the audience knows is not analogous fails the test."

### Item 11.2
**Q:** The Tupperware home-party model is the canonical example of Liking. What's the structural move?

- **Emerging:** "Friends sell to friends."
- **Developing:** "The host's liking transfers to the product."
- **Secure:** "Transferring the request from a stranger (Tupperware, a brand) to a friend (the party host, an established trusted relationship). The audience isn't really evaluating Tupperware — they're evaluating their friend who's hosting. The principle's lever is the pre-existing relationship; the product rides along. Honest version: the host actually likes and uses the product. Manipulative version: the host is incentivised to sell regardless of personal experience."

### Item 11.3
**Q:** Why can compliments be a weak Liking move when overdone?

- **Emerging:** "Annoying."
- **Developing:** "They feel fake."
- **Secure:** "Once the audience detects the engineering ('this salesperson is laying it on thick'), the move shifts the audience from peripheral processing (mild liking, low resistance) to central processing (active manipulation-detection, high resistance). Same principle, asymmetric direction. The craft is calibrated authenticity — real, specific, restrained — not maximum density."

## OUT-12 — Authority recognition vs credential-theatre

### Item 12.1
**Q:** What's the difference between earned authority and credential-theatre?

- **Emerging:** "One's real, one's fake."
- **Developing:** "Earned has actual expertise; theatre has just the cues."
- **Secure:** "Earned authority — verifiable expertise relevant to the specific claim (cardiologist on heart disease, climate scientist on warming). Credential-theatre — authority cues without underlying competence relevant to the claim (cardiologist on financial advice, PhD in unrelated field cited for the title alone). Honest practitioners disclose the limits of their authority. Manipulators inflate them by association with unrelated credentials."

### Item 12.2
**Q:** Why did the Milgram experiments work? What was the authority cue?

- **Emerging:** "People obey authority."
- **Developing:** "The lab coat and the institutional setting."
- **Secure:** "The lab coat and the institutional setting (Yale, scientific research) — symbolic authority cues operating on peripheral processing. The 'authority' had no actual expertise relevant to administering electric shocks. Participants weren't evaluating the figure's competence; they were responding to the *symbolic* authority. The result demonstrated how strong the peripheral cue is — strong enough to override moral judgment in many participants. Modern instances: men in suits with clipboards getting compliance the same suit-and-clipboard would not get on the street."

### Item 12.3
**Q:** A wellness influencer with 2M Instagram followers and a self-published health book recommends a supplement. Authority?

- **Emerging:** "Yes — they're an expert."
- **Developing:** "Symbolic, not earned."
- **Secure:** "Symbolic authority dressed as earned. The cues — large following, published book — read as authority via peripheral processing. The underlying competence (peer-reviewed credentials relevant to the supplement's claim, conflict-of-interest disclosure, clinical trial knowledge) is usually absent. Sophisticated audiences distinguish the two. Cialdini's framing: the symbol is real but the substance isn't. Manipulation when the audience is trusting the symbol thinking it's substance."

## OUT-13 — Commitment + escalation traps

### Item 13.1
**Q:** What's the foot-in-the-door technique?

- **Emerging:** "Pushy sales."
- **Developing:** "Get a small yes first, then ask for more."
- **Secure:** "Secure a small initial commitment that the audience accepts easily. Once made, the commitment reshapes how the audience sees themselves ('I am the kind of person who said yes to that'). The next ask, even if substantially larger, pulls consistency pressure with the prior commitment. The classic case: residents who agreed to a small window sign supporting safe driving were dramatically more likely to host a large ugly billboard on their lawn weeks later, vs. residents asked cold."

### Item 13.2
**Q:** Subscription services that make signup easy and cancellation hard are exploiting which principle, and how?

- **Emerging:** "Commitment."
- **Developing:** "Foot in the door — once you're in, you stay."
- **Secure:** "Commitment / Consistency, weaponised through asymmetric friction. The initial commitment was small (free trial, easy signup); consistency pressure plus the loss-aversion pull of 'cancel and lose access' raises the cost of leaving. Dark-pattern UX (roach-motel cancellation flow, phone-only cancellation, retention pop-ups) compounds the consistency principle with deliberate friction. The principle is real; the *engineered* asymmetry is the manipulation."

### Item 13.3
**Q:** When does Commitment / Consistency NOT pressure the audience to follow through?

- **Emerging:** "When they don't want to."
- **Developing:** "When the original commitment wasn't really voluntary."
- **Secure:** "Cialdini's three conditions: (a) the commitment must have been *voluntary* — coerced commitments don't generate consistency pressure; (b) *public* — private commitments are weaker; (c) *effortful* — costless commitments generate weak pressure. Strip any of the three and the principle weakens. A box you ticked without reading isn't a real commitment in the principle's sense."

## OUT-14 — Cross-cultural calibration

### Item 14.1
**Q:** How does Authority work differently in high vs low power-distance cultures?

- **Emerging:** "It's the same."
- **Developing:** "Some cultures respect authority more."
- **Secure:** "Hofstede's power-distance dimension. High power-distance cultures (e.g. many East Asian, Middle Eastern contexts) respond more strongly to formal authority cues — titles, hierarchical position, institutional credentials. Low power-distance cultures (e.g. Nordic) respond less; expertise must be demonstrated, not just credentialed. Practical implication: an authority-led pitch that works in Singapore may underperform in Sweden, even with identical content."

### Item 14.2
**Q:** How does reciprocity timing vary cross-culturally?

- **Emerging:** "It doesn't."
- **Developing:** "Some cultures expect quicker repayment."
- **Secure:** "The acceptable interval between favour and return varies — some cultures expect near-immediate reciprocation; others extend the obligation over months or years (relationship-based gift exchange cycles). A 'I gave you this, now you owe me' framing felt as manipulative in a long-timing culture may be standard practice in a short-timing one. Practical implication: international B2B contexts especially require calibration; what reads as professional in one culture reads as transactional in another."

### Item 14.3
**Q:** Why does the Unity principle work differently in collectivist vs individualist cultures?

- **Emerging:** "It doesn't."
- **Developing:** "Group identity matters more in collectivist places."
- **Secure:** "Unity activates the 'we' identity — the relevant group is broader and more durable in collectivist cultures (extended family, regional identity, professional clan operating across decades). In individualist cultures the 'we' tends to be narrower and more situational. A unity-based pitch ('as fellow alumni / as parents / as people from this city') lands harder in collectivist contexts. Risk: fabricated unity reads as exploitation in either context, but the threshold for what feels authentic is lower in individualist cultures."

## OUT-15 — Unity

### Item 15.1
**Q:** What distinguishes Unity from Liking — they sound similar?

- **Emerging:** "Same thing."
- **Developing:** "Unity is stronger group identity."
- **Secure:** "Liking operates on individual-to-individual attraction (you like this person, you defer more). Unity operates on shared identity (you and this person are part of the same 'we'). Family members donate kidneys at rates that Liking alone couldn't explain — the shared identity activates a different mechanism than personal liking. Unity is more durable because shared identity is harder to fabricate than likeability."

### Item 15.2
**Q:** A political candidate frames a pitch as "we, as people who grew up in this town, know that..." What principle, and what's the move?

- **Emerging:** "Just connecting with the audience."
- **Developing:** "Unity — invoking shared identity."
- **Secure:** "Unity. Move: framing the pitch from a shared-identity vantage rather than a persuader-to-audience one. When the shared identity is real (the candidate did grow up there, the audience does identify), the move is honest and powerful. When the identity is fabricated or trivially shared, it reads as condescension and produces backlash. Most political misuse of Unity is in the manufacture-the-shared-identity zone."

### Item 15.3
**Q:** A wellness brand opens marketing copy with "we, as women, deserve..." How would you evaluate whether this is honest Unity or manufactured tribalism?

- **Emerging:** "Sounds nice."
- **Developing:** "Depends on whether the brand is actually run by women."
- **Secure:** "Two tests: (a) is the shared identity *relevant* to the claim — does being a woman actually bear on the product's value, or is the identity being invoked to lend authority to an unrelated claim? (b) is the brand's positioning consistent with the identity it's invoking, or is the 'we' rhetoric a marketing layer over a generic offer? Honest Unity passes both. Manufactured tribalism uses identity as a wrapper for ordinary commerce."

## OUT-16 — Application + misuse roundup

### Item 16.1
**Q:** Walk me through an ad or sales email you saw recently. Name the principles deployed, judge each as honest or manipulative, and say what you'd change.

- **Emerging:** Names 1–2 principles loosely, doesn't apply truth-test.
- **Developing:** Names 3–4 principles, partial truth-test, suggests improvements that move some toward honesty.
- **Secure:** Names principles precisely (often catching stacking), applies truth-test to each, suggests changes that preserve persuasive effect while moving the deployment toward honest amplification. Notices the cumulative ethical weight of multiple principles deployed together.

### Item 16.2
**Q:** You've been asked to redesign a SaaS pricing page. You can use any of Cialdini's seven principles. How do you decide which?

- **Emerging:** "Use them all."
- **Developing:** "Pick whichever fits the offer."
- **Secure:** "Audience-first. (a) Who is the visitor — likely peripheral (early evaluation) or central (deep eval)? (b) What truth do you actually have to amplify — is the company a real authority? Are there real peers using it? Is supply or time genuinely constrained? (c) What stacks credibly without contradicting itself? Social Proof + Authority work well together when both are real. Scarcity stacked with Liking can read as pushy. The selection is downstream of what's actually true about your offer."

### Item 16.3
**Q:** Name the three most common manipulation patterns you'll encounter in the wild, after this course.

- **Emerging:** Names 1–2, vague.
