# Persuasion & Sales — Glossary

> **Document type:** REFERENCE · **Audience:** learner · **Default category:** vocabulary
>
> Flat term glossary for the Seducing Strangers (Persuasion & Sales) course. Cialdini's seven principles, ELM concepts, ethics distinctions, and 10+ key terms from the practitioner and academic literatures. All entries are HFF-authored definitions; named frameworks (SUCCESS, Prospect Theory, etc.) are attributed but explained in HFF's own words.

---

## The Seven Cialdini Principles

**Reciprocity** — The unbalanced internal pressure people feel to repay favours, gifts, concessions, or information given to them. Operates regardless of whether the original favour was requested. The first of Cialdini's seven principles, with cross-cultural anthropological evidence as a near-universal foundation of human cooperation.

**Commitment and Consistency** — Once people make a commitment — particularly one that is public, voluntary, and effortful — they feel pressure to behave consistently with it, even when conditions change. Drives the "foot in the door" technique and the consistency pressure behind subscription escalation patterns.

**Social Proof** — People determine what is correct by looking at what others are doing. Strongest under uncertainty, when the "others" are similar to the audience, and when there is evidence of voluntary action. Includes the descriptive-vs-injunctive norm distinction (see entries).

**Liking** — People are more easily persuaded by those they like. Liking is driven by physical attractiveness, similarity, compliments, repeated exposure, and cooperation toward a shared goal. The "Tupperware party" mechanism — transferring the request from a stranger to a friend.

**Authority** — People defer to authority figures more than the actual merit of their claims warrants. Operates on both substantive expertise (real domain authority) and symbolic cues (titles, uniforms, credentials). Distinguishable as earned authority vs credential-theatre.

**Scarcity** — People value things more when they are scarce. Activates loss aversion (see Prospect Theory entry). Cialdini's most credibility-fragile principle — once an audience detects fake scarcity, all other claims on the site are tainted.

**Unity** — The seventh principle, added in the 2021 edition of *Influence*. People are most influenced by those they share an identity with — family, ethnic group, professional community, regional identity, shared adversity. More durable than Liking because identity is harder to fabricate than likeability.

## Cognitive Frame — Two Modes of Processing

**ELM (Elaboration Likelihood Model)** — Petty & Cacioppo's (1986) model of persuasion as operating via two routes:

**Central Route Processing** — Deliberate, evidence-weighing, slow. The audience is motivated and able to evaluate the merit of the claim directly. Persuasion via the central route requires substantive content; peripheral cues are weak here.

**Peripheral Route Processing** — Heuristic, cue-driven, fast. The audience is processing with low elaboration — usually because of low motivation, limited attention, or low ability to evaluate the substantive content. Cialdini's seven principles operate primarily on the peripheral route. They weaken (but do not disappear) when the audience shifts toward central processing.

**Practical implication** — A B2B procurement team running a deep evaluation is harder to persuade with peripheral cues than a consumer in a checkout flow. The principles still work; the strongest effects come on peripheral-route audiences.

## Loss / Scarcity Asymmetry

**Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky 1979)** — The finding that losses feel roughly twice as painful as equivalent gains feel pleasurable. A frame of *you might lose this* recruits this asymmetry; a frame of *you might gain this* does not. Same economic message, asymmetric pull. Underpins the Scarcity principle and the choice of loss-framing vs gain-framing in persuasion copy.

**Loss Aversion** — The asymmetric weighting Prospect Theory documents. Approximately 2:1 in laboratory studies (varies by domain, individual, and stake size).

**Loss Frame vs Gain Frame** — Two ways to present the same offer. *Gain frame:* "Save $50 if you sign up today." *Loss frame:* "You'll lose $50 if you wait." Same economic content; the loss frame typically pulls more action because of loss aversion. The choice depends on audience sophistication and the underlying truth of the urgency claim.

## Practitioner Frameworks

**Attention → Attraction → Action (Weltman 2015)** — Weltman's overarching arc for any persuasion task. Attention is the threshold problem (being seen at all). Attraction is the seduction phase (making the offer feel desirable). Action is the close (the matched ask). Most persuasion failures, in Weltman's diagnosis, are not failures at the action stage; they are failures at attention or attraction that the action stage cannot rescue.

**The Four Questions (Weltman 2015)** — A diagnostic for any persuasion artefact: Who is talking and why should I listen? What are they offering? How does it work? Why does it matter — why now, why me? When persuasion is failing, check which question the audience cannot answer from the material in front of them.

**Make the Unfamiliar Familiar (Weltman 2015)** — The move of introducing something new by anchoring it to something the audience already understands and values. Activates Liking via association. Misuse pattern: false analogies that imply similarity to a beloved reference that does not actually hold ("we're just like Apple" when the resemblance is superficial).

**SUCCESS Framework (Heath & Heath 2007)** — A six-dimension checklist for whether an idea will stick: **S**imple, **U**nexpected, **C**oncrete, **C**redible, **E**motional, **S**tories. Used in this course as the application checklist when the learner builds their own persuasion artefact in the application module.

## Social Proof — Sub-concepts

**Descriptive Norm** — A statement of what people *do*. "10,000 customers buy this every month." Used carelessly, descriptive norms can backfire when the behaviour is undesired (the Petrified Forest boomerang case — see entry).

**Injunctive Norm** — A statement of what people *should* do. "Please leave the wood in the park." Often more effective than descriptive norms for behaviour the practitioner wants to *change*.

**Petrified Forest Boomerang Case** — Cialdini et al. (2006) finding that a sign reading "many past visitors have removed wood from the park" — intended as a warning — actually *increased* theft. The descriptive norm signalled "this is what people do" and normalised the undesired behaviour. The injunctive norm variant was more effective. The case is taught in the course as a cautionary example: getting descriptive and injunctive framing collision wrong reverses the intended effect.

## Authority — Sub-concepts

**Earned Authority** — Authority derived from verifiable expertise relevant to the specific claim. A doctor speaking on medicine, a lawyer on law, a published researcher on their domain. Honest practitioners disclose the limits of their authority.

**Credential-Theatre** — Authority cues without underlying competence relevant to the claim. Vague titles ("industry expert"), unrelated PhDs, name-dropped associations the practitioner had loose connection with. The misuse pattern of the Authority principle.

**Symbolic Authority** — Authority cues (uniforms, suits, official-looking documents, formatted credentials) operating on the audience's peripheral processing. Symbolic authority can be honest (the doctor wears a lab coat because they actually are a doctor) or theatrical (the salesman wears a lab coat because lab coats sell). The course's posture is that symbolic authority is acceptable when the symbol reflects underlying substance.

## Ethics Distinctions

**Ethical Persuasion** — Use of the persuasion principles to *amplify* something true about the offer. The audience would still agree with the proposition if they could see exactly what was being done and why. Passes Cialdini's truth-test and Weltman's would-you-be-comfortable-explaining test.

**Manipulation** — Use of the persuasion principles to *manufacture* something the audience would reject if they could see behind the curtain. Fake scarcity, manufactured testimonials, manipulative reciprocity, credential-theatre, fabricated unity. Fails the truth-test.

**The Truth-Test** — The operational test for ethical persuasion used in this course. "Would the audience still be persuaded if they could see exactly what was being done and why?" Yes → ethical. No → manipulation. Phrased equivalently as Weltman's "would you be comfortable explaining to the audience exactly what you were doing and why?"

**Dark Patterns** — A term from UX/design for interface choices that exploit cognitive shortcuts against the user's interest. Overlap with manipulation in the persuasion vocabulary. Examples: confirmshaming, hidden costs, roach-motel cancellation flows, fake countdown timers. Recognised by the course as the misuse cluster of multiple principles operating together.

**Credibility Budget** — A practitioner's reservoir of audience trust, depleted by manipulation and rebuilt by honest persuasion. Cialdini's central long-game argument: manipulation cashes in the credibility budget for short-term gain; honest persuasion compounds it. Once spent, credibility is slow and expensive to rebuild.

## Cross-Cultural Calibration

**Power Distance (Hofstede)** — Cultural dimension measuring how much a society accepts hierarchical authority. High power-distance cultures (e.g. many East Asian, Middle Eastern contexts) respond more strongly to formal Authority cues; low power-distance cultures (e.g. Nordic) respond less. Calibrate Authority deployments accordingly.

**Reciprocity Timing Norms** — The acceptable interval between favour and return varies cross-culturally. Some cultures expect immediate reciprocation; others extend the obligation over months or years. A manipulative-feeling "I gave you this, now you owe me" framing in one culture may be appropriate timing in another.

**Individualism vs Collectivism** — Hofstede dimension. Affects Social Proof (collectivist cultures respond more strongly to group descriptive norms) and Unity (the relevant "we" group is broader in collectivist contexts). Affects Commitment / Consistency differently (face-saving consistency pressures vary by collectivism).

## Other Key Terms

**Compliance** — The general field Cialdini studied. The professional practice of getting people to agree to requests. Encompasses sales, fundraising, recruitment, advertising, political persuasion, and similar.

**Pre-Suasion (Cialdini 2016)** — The follow-up book to *Influence*. Argues that the *moment* immediately before a persuasion attempt can be shaped to make the audience more receptive. Not central to this course but referenced in passing.

**Heuristic** — A fast, low-effort mental shortcut for decision-making. Cialdini's principles all exploit heuristics. The brain runs heuristics because evaluating every decision from first principles would be paralysing.

**Foot-in-the-Door** — A specific commitment-consistency technique: secure a small initial agreement, then escalate to the real ask. Classic application of the Commitment principle. Often crosses into manipulation when the escalation is steeper than the initial agreement implied consent to.

**Door-in-the-Face** — The reciprocity-based reverse: open with an unreasonable ask the audience will refuse, then "concede" to a more reasonable ask. The audience feels a reciprocity pressure to match the concession. Effective short-term; carries manipulation risk if the initial ask was never serious.
