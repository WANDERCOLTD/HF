# Course Architect brief — Big Five (OCEAN) Personality

> Worked example generated by applying the `course-architect` agent loop manually. This is the *first* brief produced through the new agent's process and serves as a reference for what the output shape looks like end-to-end.

## 1. Audience-purpose

- **Audience:** Non-AI startup adult attending a 1-hour demo session, no prior experience using AI in their work.
- **Post-course use:** Dinner-party / self-knowledge currency. The learner walks away able to read a friend's behaviour through OCEAN, recognise their own profile honestly, and call out the common folk mistakes ("introvert = shy").
- **Demo constraints:** Voice-only (VAPI). Target 5 calls, ~7 minutes each. Wow moment required by call 2 (memory) and call 3 (catch-and-correct).

## 2. Candidates considered

- **Big Five (OCEAN)** — Native source-to-audience match, 5-trait taxonomy is both academia and the audience's native vocabulary.
- **Attachment Theory** — Strong contender; covered in its own brief. Stronger emotional pull, slightly heavier cultural-validity caveat.
- **Dark Triad** — Stronger dinner-party currency; weaker on "this could actually change how I see myself" — Dark Triad is about *other people*, not self-knowledge.

## 3. Source quality (leading candidate)

| Source | Trust level | Role |
|---|---|---|
| John & Srivastava (1999) — *"The Big Five Trait Taxonomy"* in *Handbook of Personality* (2e), ch. 4 | `PUBLISHED_REFERENCE` (L3) | **Spine.** Defines 5 domains; traces lexical-hypothesis history; explains cross-linguistic emergence. |
| Soto & John (2017) — *"The next Big Five Inventory (BFI-2): Developing and assessing a hierarchical model with 15 facets"* (*JPSP*, 113(1), 117–143) | `PUBLISHED_REFERENCE` (L3) | **Facet structure.** 5 × 3 = 15 facets. Validated items = LO seed. |
| Costa & McCrae (1992) — *NEO-PI-R Professional Manual* | `ACCREDITED_MATERIAL` (L4) | Alternative 6-facet-per-domain shape if more granularity wanted. |
| McCrae & Costa (1997) — *"Personality trait structure as a human universal"* (*American Psychologist*, 52(5), 509–516) | `PUBLISHED_REFERENCE` (L3) | Cross-cultural validity; answers "is this just Western psychology?" |
| OpenStax Psychology 2e, ch. 11 (Personality) | `PUBLISHED_REFERENCE` (L3), public-domain | Plain-English Content Authority excerpt, suitable for inline quotation in the composed prompt. |

**No `UNVERIFIED` sources used.** The 16Personalities / MBTI overlap is *not* used as a structural reference — it's a pop translation of (poorly mapped) Big Five and confuses learners. If the agent or tutor surfaces MBTI, treat as a misconception to catch.

## 4. Structure-match check

**Native match.** No translation tax.

- Source-led shape: 5 domains × 3 facets (BFI-2). 15 facets + 5 domain LOs + history/cross-cultural meta = clean tree.
- Audience-led shape: "What's my type? What are the 5 traits? What do they mean for me in real life?"

Both want the same 5-trait spine with depth into a few facets. Audience does not want a 30-facet NEO-PI-R deep dive (overkill); does want enough facet depth to feel surprising ("Sociability ≠ Assertiveness").

## 5. Bottom-up validation — 3 sampled LOs

### LO sample 1: `ocean.extraversion`

- **Definition:** Extraversion is the trait reflecting social engagement, assertiveness, and energy level — composed of three facets (Sociability, Assertiveness, Energy Level) in BFI-2 (Soto & John 2017).
- **Recall items (from BFI-2 instrument):**
  1. "True or false: BFI-2 separates being talkative from being assertive into two different facets." → True (Sociability vs Assertiveness)
  2. "If someone scores high on Assertiveness but low on Sociability, what does that look like in real life?" → Strong opinions/leadership without needing parties; quiet leader profile.
  3. "What's the third facet of Extraversion alongside Sociability and Assertiveness?" → Energy Level (sometimes called Activity).
- **Misconception probe:** "Many people equate Extraversion with 'liking parties.' What's wrong with that mapping?" → Conflates one facet (Sociability) with the whole trait; ignores Assertiveness and Energy Level which can be high without party-going.

| Test | Result |
|---|---|
| Citable | ✅ Soto & John 2017 BFI-2 items + trait definitions |
| Discriminative | ✅ Probes E, not adjacent traits |
| Voice-deliverable | ✅ All items ≤8 seconds spoken |
| Misconception-targeted | ✅ "Extravert = party person" is documented in the literature as the most common folk error |

### LO sample 2: `ocean.negative_emotionality`

- **Definition:** Negative Emotionality (formerly "Neuroticism" — renamed in BFI-2) reflects tendency toward Anxiety, Depression, and Emotional Volatility (Soto & John 2017).
- **Recall items:**
  1. "What did Soto and John rename 'Neuroticism' to in BFI-2, and why?" → Negative Emotionality; the original term was clinically loaded and morally judgmental in colloquial use.
  2. "Name the three facets of Negative Emotionality." → Anxiety, Depression, Emotional Volatility.
  3. "Trait Anxiety vs Emotional Volatility — what's the distinction?" → Trait Anxiety = persistent worry baseline; Volatility = how *much* mood shifts in response to events. Two different things — can have one without the other.
- **Misconception probe:** "Is high Negative Emotionality the same as being mentally ill?" → No — it's a normal trait distribution. Clinical anxiety/depression are different constructs assessed by different instruments (DSM/ICD criteria, not BFI-2).

| Test | Result |
|---|---|
| Citable | ✅ Soto & John 2017 explicit rename + facet structure |
| Discriminative | ✅ Distinguishes within-trait facets |
| Voice-deliverable | ✅ |
| Misconception-targeted | ✅ "Neurotic = neurotic" trait-disorder confusion is the documented misuse this rename addresses |

### LO sample 3: `ocean.agreeableness`

- **Definition:** Agreeableness reflects Compassion, Respectfulness, and Trust — *not* general "niceness" (Soto & John 2017).
- **Recall items:**
  1. "Why is high Agreeableness *not* the same as being nice?" → Agreeableness is about valuing harmony, others' welfare, and assuming positive intent; "nice" conflates with social skill, conflict avoidance, and likeability. High-A people can disagree fiercely with respect; "nice" people can be high in *low* trust.
  2. "Trust as a facet — what does context do to it?" → Trust is more context-sensitive than other facets; can be domain-specific (high in close relationships, low in professional context).
  3. "How does Agreeableness predict workplace behaviour?" → Modest predictor of cooperation, less of performance; high-A people are better at conflict resolution but often underpaid (Judge et al. 2012).
- **Misconception probe:** "Your colleague never says no and never pushes back — high Agreeableness?" → Not necessarily. Could be low Assertiveness (Extraversion facet) or high Negative Emotionality (conflict-avoidance from anxiety). Agreeableness specifically is about *valuing* harmony, not avoiding it.

| Test | Result |
|---|---|
| Citable | ✅ |
| Discriminative | ✅ Catches the common conflation with Extraversion/Negative-Emotionality facets |
| Voice-deliverable | ✅ |
| Misconception-targeted | ✅ "Agreeable = nice" is documented |

**Conclusion:** Bottom-up validation passes cleanly. 3/3 LOs survive all 4 tests. Other 12 LOs follow the same shape.

## 6. Top-down validation

**Course outcome:** *"After 5 calls (~35 min), the learner can (a) name the five OCEAN traits and the BFI-2 facets within each, (b) honestly self-describe their own likely profile on each trait, and (c) catch three common folk misuses of the taxonomy in everyday speech (Extravert = party person; Neurotic = neurotic; Agreeable = nice)."*

LO ladder check:
- 15 facet LOs → cover the (a) naming layer
- 5 domain-meaning LOs (one per trait, covering definition + classic misconception) → cover the (b) self-description layer
- 2 application LOs (cross-cultural validity; reading combinations) → cover the (c) folk-error layer

= 16 LOs total → comfortably above the EMA-visibility floor (~15). Outcome ladders.

## 7. Failure-mode sweep

**Codifiable checks:**

1. **Source-authority risk:** ✅ PASS. All sources L3 or higher.
2. **Voice/visual mismatch:** ✅ PASS. No diagrams required; all material delivers in spoken form.
3. **EMA mastery invisibility:** ✅ PASS. 16 LOs across 5 calls = ~3 LO mastery updates per call → graph fills visibly. Recommend nudging `Playbook.config.skillScoringEmaHalfLifeDays` to 4 for the demo to make movement crisp.
4. **Teaching profile compatibility:** ✅ PASS. Match: `recall-led` for naming/facets + `discussion-led` overrides for the "what does this mean in life" reflection layer. Combine via `teachingOverrides`. Custom `deliveryHints`: "for each trait, after naming the facets, ask the learner for one concrete example from their own life before moving on."
5. **`INSTRUCTION_CATEGORIES` collision:** ✅ PASS. All proposed LOs have a learner-visible facet (`learnerVisible=true` per `lib/curriculum/lo-audience.ts`).

**Judgment calls:**

6. **Pop-folklore creep:** **FLAG.** MBTI / 16Personalities is everywhere and overlaps imperfectly with Big Five. Strategy: explicitly *name* MBTI in the meta-LO as the popular alternative, explain why academia uses Big Five instead (better cross-cultural validity, no forced categorisation, evidence base) — turn the folk knowledge into a teachable contrast, not a competitor.
7. **Demo audience tune-out:** ✅ PASS. "What's my type?" is a perennial adult interest hook.
8. **Misconception density:** ✅ HIGH. Documented misconceptions on every trait — especially "introvert = shy", "neurotic = clinically neurotic", "agreeable = nice", "open = artsy". Perfect Socratic catch-and-correct surface.
9. **Cross-cultural validity:** **MINOR FLAG.** Big Five replicates cross-culturally better than most personality models (McCrae & Costa 1997), but is not universal in non-WEIRD samples. Address in the final application LO; don't pretend it's settled.

**Net:** No blockers. Two flags both convert into teachable content rather than course rejection.

## 8. Demo arc (call-by-call)

- **Call 1 (Foundations + Module 1):** Meta-LO + Open-Mindedness. Tutor walks through Allport→Cattell→Goldberg lexical history (1-minute hook), then introduces O with its 3 facets. **Wow:** "There are 18,000 personality adjectives in English. Across cultures, languages, and 50 years of research, they collapse into the same 5 dimensions. That's not psychology guessing — that's data."
- **Call 2 (Conscientiousness):** C, its 3 facets, and its job-performance predictive power. **Wow ("it remembered me"):** Opens with `priorCallRecap` — "Last call you said you score high on Aesthetic Sensitivity but low on Intellectual Curiosity. Interesting combination — most people who score high on one score high on the other. Want to dig into that?"
- **Call 3 (Extraversion):** E, the Sociability/Assertiveness/Energy split. **Wow ("it caught my error"):** Learner says "I'm an introvert" — tutor probes facet by facet, learner discovers they're low Sociability + high Assertiveness + medium Energy = "quiet leader" profile, not classical introvert. Their mental model updates in real time.
- **Call 4 (Agreeableness + Negative Emotionality):** A and N together. Tutor draws the contrast — A is about *valuing* harmony, N is about *being unsettled by* threat. Common to conflate; they predict different behaviours.
- **Call 5 (Reading Profiles + Cross-cultural application):** Combinations (high-C + high-N looks very different from high-C + low-N). **Wow (dashboard moment):** Learner sees mastery graph filled out — 14/16 LOs green. Tutor surfaces the 2 weakest LOs as "if you want to push further on your own, look at these two facets next."

Module 4: Agreeableness + Neg. Emotionality  (4 LOs)
  ├── ocean.agreeableness            — Trait A — and why high-A ≠ "nice"

  ├── ocean.negative_emotionality    — Trait N: the rename from "Neuroticism" and why it matters
  └── ocean.volatility_vs_anxiety    — Facets: emotional volatility ≠ trait anxiety
