# Course Architect brief — Attachment Theory

> Second worked example through the `course-architect` loop, on a contrasting topic shape (discussion-led instead of recall-led; reflective instead of taxonomic).

## 1. Audience-purpose

- **Audience:** Non-AI startup adult at demo, voice-only. Same audience as the Big Five brief — so the comparison is content-driven, not audience-driven.
- **Post-course use:** Self-knowledge in close relationships (partner, family, close colleagues). The learner walks away able to (a) recognise their own dominant attachment pattern under stress, (b) distinguish the four canonical styles (Ainsworth 1978 + Hazan & Shaver 1987), (c) avoid the most common armchair-diagnoses ("avoidant = cold heartless person").
- **Demo constraints:** Voice-only (VAPI), 5 calls × ~7 min. Wow by call 2 (memory), call 3 (catch-and-correct), call 5 (dashboard).

## 2. Candidates considered

- **Attachment Theory** — Strong emotional pull; the topic almost everyone has a personal stake in. Universally interesting at adult demos.
- **Big Five** — Stronger as a *taxonomy*; weaker as an *emotional hook*. Covered in its own brief.
- **Stages of Grief (Kübler-Ross)** — Initially attractive but academic support for the "stage" structure has weakened substantially (Stroebe et al. 2017). Would have to be taught as "the popular model and where it's wrong" — net negative for a *demonstration* of the system.

## 3. Source quality (leading candidate)

| Source | Trust level | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Bowlby (1969/1973/1980) — *Attachment* trilogy (Hogarth Press / Basic Books) | `PUBLISHED_REFERENCE` (L3) | Theoretical foundation: attachment as a biologically rooted system, internal working models. |
| Ainsworth, Blehar, Waters & Wall (1978) — *Patterns of Attachment: A Psychological Study of the Strange Situation* (Erlbaum) | `PUBLISHED_REFERENCE` (L3) | **Spine.** The original 4-pattern classification: Secure, Anxious-Ambivalent, Avoidant, Disorganized (Main & Solomon 1990 for the fourth). |
| Hazan & Shaver (1987) — *"Romantic love conceptualized as an attachment process"* (*JPSP*, 52(3), 511–524) | `PUBLISHED_REFERENCE` (L3) | The adult-attachment extension that gives the demo its dinner-party currency. |
| Mikulincer & Shaver (2016) — *Attachment in Adulthood: Structure, Dynamics, and Change* (2e, Guilford) | `PUBLISHED_REFERENCE` (L3) | **Canonical contemporary textbook.** Adult patterns, change across life, evidence base. |
| Rothbaum, Weisz, Pott, Miyake & Morelli (2000) — *"Attachment and culture: Security in the United States and Japan"* (*American Psychologist*, 55(10), 1093–1104) | `PUBLISHED_REFERENCE` (L3) | Cross-cultural caveat: what looks like avoidance in one culture is securely accommodative in another. Needed for the final module. |

**Sources to actively *not* use as structural references:** Instagram-therapy "attachment style quiz" content, "anxious-avoidant trap" pop frameworks (often informed by Levine & Heller's *Attached* (2010) which mixes academic and popular framing). These are `UNVERIFIED` (L0) when used as the spine. They make excellent *misconceptions to catch and correct*, however.

## 4. Structure-match check

**Native match with one wrinkle.**

- Source-led shape: 4 patterns (Secure, Anxious, Avoidant, Disorganized), formation in infancy, extension to adulthood, internal working models.
- Audience-led shape: "What's my attachment style? What does my partner's mean?"

Match is native — *but* the audience-led shape tends toward typology ("I'm anxious-avoidant"), and the literature treats attachment as a *continuous* dimensional construct (cf. Fraley & Waller 1998) overlaid on the categorical typology. The brief needs an explicit LO addressing dimensions vs categories or the course teaches a fixed-typology error.

## 5. Bottom-up validation — 3 sampled LOs

### LO sample 1: `attach.secure`

- **Definition:** Secure attachment is characterised by comfort with closeness and autonomy, baseline trust that others are responsive in distress, and the capacity to seek support without anxiety (Ainsworth et al. 1978; Mikulincer & Shaver 2016 ch. 1).
- **Recall items:**
  1. "In the Strange Situation, what behaviour distinguished securely-attached infants from insecure ones?" → Secure infants used the caregiver as a *base*: explored when she was present, were distressed at separation, and were *easily comforted on reunion*. Insecure infants either avoided reunion or alternated approach/anger.
  2. "Hazan and Shaver translated infant Secure attachment to adult relationships. What does it look like?" → Comfort with intimacy *and* independence, low anxiety about partner's responsiveness, ability to ask for support without games.
  3. "Is Secure attachment the same as 'never having relationship problems'?" → No — securely attached people have conflicts, but they repair them. Security is about *response to distress*, not absence of distress.
- **Misconception probe:** "If someone never seems anxious in their relationship, are they Secure?" → Not necessarily. Could be Avoidant (suppressing rather than feeling). The distinction is what they do *under stress*, not their baseline calm.

| Test | Result |
|---|---|
| Citable | ✅ Ainsworth 1978 Strange Situation + Hazan & Shaver 1987 |
| Discriminative | ✅ Probes Secure vs Avoidant directly |
| Voice-deliverable | ✅ |
| Misconception-targeted | ✅ "Secure = no anxiety" is documented |

### LO sample 2: `attach.avoidant`

- **Definition:** Avoidant attachment (dismissing in adulthood) is a deactivation strategy — under attachment-related stress, the system *down-regulates* expressions of need. Internal working model: "I don't need others; vulnerability is unsafe" (Mikulincer & Shaver 2016 ch. 3).
- **Recall items:**
  1. "What does an Avoidant adult do when their partner reaches for emotional closeness?" → Withdraws, deflects, intellectualises, or creates distance ("I need space"). Critically: the *internal* physiology often shows arousal even when *externally* they appear cool (deactivation, not absence of distress).
  2. "What's the difference between Avoidant attachment and 'being introverted'?" → Introversion is about social energy/stimulation preference. Avoidance is about *intimacy regulation under stress*. Introverts can be deeply Secure; Avoidants can be highly social.
  3. "Why is Avoidant attachment often missed in self-report?" → Avoidants are skilled at not noticing their own attachment-related distress; self-reports often look 'fine' until you measure physiology (skin conductance, cortisol) or behaviour under acute stress.
- **Misconception probe:** "Your friend ghosts people they're dating. Avoidant?" → Maybe — but ghosting could also be Disorganized (overwhelmed by approach/avoid conflict) or low Conscientiousness (Big Five), or situational. The behavior is consistent with Avoidance but doesn't prove it.

| Test | Result |
|---|---|
| Citable | ✅ Mikulincer & Shaver 2016, Cassidy & Kobak 1988 (deactivation) |
| Discriminative | ✅ Pulls apart from introversion explicitly |
| Voice-deliverable | ✅ |
| Misconception-targeted | ✅ "Avoidant = cold/heartless" is the central armchair-diagnosis trap |

### LO sample 3: `attach.styles_are_dimensional`

- **Definition:** Although Ainsworth's typology gives 4 named patterns, modern research treats attachment as 2 continuous dimensions — *anxiety* (about responsiveness) and *avoidance* (of closeness) — on which a person sits anywhere, not in a box (Fraley & Waller 1998; Brennan, Clark & Shaver 1998 ECR).
- **Recall items:**
  1. "What are the two dimensions modern attachment research measures (per Fraley & Waller 1998)?" → Anxiety (how worried about responsiveness) and Avoidance (how much closeness they tolerate).
  2. "If both dimensions are *low*, what category does the person sit in?" → Secure.
  3. "Can a person's position on these dimensions change across life?" → Yes — attachment is moderately stable but moves with relationship experiences (Mikulincer & Shaver 2016 ch. 6).
- **Misconception probe:** "I took a quiz and got 'Anxious-Avoidant.' Is that one of the four styles?" → It's Disorganized in the original typology, but in modern continuous-dimension framing it just means 'high on both anxiety and avoidance.' The Instagram-quiz typology mixes vocabulary.

| Test | Result |
|---|---|
| Citable | ✅ Fraley & Waller 1998; Brennan et al. 1998 ECR |
| Discriminative | ✅ |
| Voice-deliverable | ✅ |
| Misconception-targeted | ✅ The "I'm anxious-avoidant" pop-culture identity is *exactly* the misconception this LO catches |

**Conclusion:** Bottom-up validation passes. The third LO sample is the linchpin that prevents the course from reinforcing a fixed-typology mental model.

## 6. Top-down validation

**Course outcome:** *"After 5 calls, the learner can (a) identify their own attachment patterns under stress in concrete terms, not Instagram-quiz labels; (b) distinguish the 4 canonical patterns and explain why dimensions matter alongside types; (c) recognise the most common armchair-diagnosis errors (avoidant = cold; anxious = needy; ghosting = avoidant)."*

LO ladder:
- 4 pattern LOs (one per style) → cover the (b) distinguish layer
- 2 mechanism LOs (Strange Situation; deactivation/hyperactivation strategies) → cover the (a) own-pattern layer
- 1 dimensions LO (continuous vs categorical) → addresses the modern-research wrinkle
- 1 origins LO (infant → adult continuity, internal working models)
- 1 change LO (does attachment change across life?)
- 1 cross-cultural LO (Rothbaum 2000)
- 1 misconceptions roundup LO (the armchair-diagnosis catalogue)
- 1 meta-LO (theoretical history: Bowlby's break from Freud, ethology, Harlow's monkeys)
- Total: **12 LOs** — slightly below the 15-LO comfort floor.

**Tune:** add 2 application LOs — "Reading patterns in close colleagues" and "Repair scripts when patterns clash" — bringing total to **14**. Still slightly under 15; nudge `skillScoringEmaHalfLifeDays` to 3 to keep mastery graph crisp. Acceptable.

## 7. Failure-mode sweep

**Codifiable:**

1. **Source-authority risk:** ✅ PASS. All sources L3.
2. **Voice/visual mismatch:** ✅ PASS. No diagrams. (Could optionally show the 2-dimension grid on the dashboard, but the *call* itself never needs one.)
3. **EMA mastery invisibility:** **FLAG — minor.** 14 LOs is below the 15 floor by one. Mitigation: nudge `skillScoringEmaHalfLifeDays` from default 14 → 3 for the demo, or add a 15th LO (e.g., split the misconceptions roundup into two). Decision: tune the half-life.
4. **Teaching profile compatibility:** ✅ PASS. **`discussion-led`** is the right fit. Topic is reflective and personal. `deliveryHints` overrides: "use the learner's own relationships as the working material — never abstract textbook vignettes alone."
5. **`INSTRUCTION_CATEGORIES` collision:** ✅ PASS. All LOs learner-visible.

**Judgment calls:**

6. **Pop-folklore creep:** **STRONG FLAG.** Instagram-therapy content has so saturated the topic that learners often arrive with confident wrong models (notably from Levine & Heller's *Attached* which is *adjacent* to academia but not it). Strategy: surface and name this in Module 1 ("you've probably done a quiz online; let's look at what the actual psychology says"). Treat pop-culture as a known starting point, not a competitor.
7. **Demo audience tune-out:** ✅ PASS. Topic is genuinely interesting to adults; doesn't read as school-y.
8. **Misconception density:** ✅ **EXTREMELY HIGH.** Possibly the highest of any precedent in the catalogue. "Avoidant = cold heartless" / "Anxious = needy" / "I have ADHD-style attachment" / "Disorganized = mentally ill" — perfect Socratic catch-and-correct surface.
9. **Cross-cultural validity:** **STRONG FLAG.** Rothbaum et al. 2000 found that what Western raters classify as "avoidant" in Japanese mother-infant dyads is securely accommodative in the local culture. The course MUST include a cross-cultural LO or it teaches Western-centric pathology.

**Net:** Slightly tighter than Big Five. Two LO-count workarounds and one cultural-validity LO are required. Workable.

## 8. Demo arc (call-by-call)

- **Call 1 (Origins + Strange Situation):** Bowlby's break from Freud (1-min hook), Ainsworth's Strange Situation, the four patterns named. **Wow:** "In a 20-minute lab procedure with 12-month-old infants, Mary Ainsworth could predict behaviour patterns that would persist into adult romantic relationships 25 years later. That's not personality testing — that's the attachment system showing up."
- **Call 2 (Secure + Anxious):** Two of the four patterns, in depth. **Wow ("it remembered me"):** `priorCallRecap` — "Last call you said you don't like 'space' in relationships — interesting, because that's a classic Anxious pattern. Let's see if it holds up under stress scenarios."
- **Call 3 (Avoidant + Disorganized):** The deactivation strategy + Disorganized as the approach-avoid contradiction. **Wow ("it caught my error"):** Learner says "my ex was a sociopath, totally avoidant." Tutor probes: "Was your ex consistently cold across contexts, or selectively cold under intimacy stress?" → learner's diagnosis dissolves into the Avoidant deactivation model, which is much more interesting.
- **Call 4 (Dimensions + Change across life):** Fraley & Waller's 2D continuous model. Why your "anxious-avoidant" Instagram quiz result needs unpacking. **Wow:** "Across the four canonical patterns, the actual *underlying* structure is just two dimensions. Your romantic history has more granular truth than four boxes."
- **Call 5 (Cross-cultural + Reading + Repair):** Rothbaum 2000 culture, reading patterns in close colleagues, repair scripts when patterns clash. **Dashboard wow:** Mastery graph 12/14 green. Tutor closes: "Two LOs you didn't quite lock — `attach.disorganized` and `attach.cross_cultural`. Worth a read on Mikulincer & Shaver ch. 4 and the Rothbaum paper if you want to push further."
