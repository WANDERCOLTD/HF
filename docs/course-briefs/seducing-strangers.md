# Course Architect brief — Seducing Strangers (Persuasion & Sales)

> Worked example generated by applying the `course-architect` agent loop. Third brief in the catalogue; first one based on a *trade book* (not a peer-reviewed academic source), which surfaces a new failure mode the catalogue should learn: **dual-use risk**.

## 1. Audience-purpose

- **Audience:** Non-AI startup adult attending a demo session (same pattern as Big Five + Attachment).
- **Post-course use:** Recognise persuasion principles in real-world sales/marketing/communication, apply them honestly in their own work, and catch manipulative misuses (theirs and others').
- **Demo constraints:** Voice-only (VAPI), 5 calls × ~7 minutes, wow by call 2 (memory) + call 3 (catch-and-correct).

## 2. Candidates considered

The user named *Seducing Strangers* explicitly, so candidate surfacing collapses to a single framing question: **single-source (Weltman alone) or compound (Weltman as audience hook + academic spine)?**

- **A — Weltman alone (single trade book as spine):** Direct match to user intent. Cohesive narrative voice. Weakness: trade book is `EXPERT_CURATED` (L2) at best; no peer-reviewed validation; Weltman's framings are persuasive but not empirically tested.
- **B — Compound: Cialdini spine + Weltman framing layer + Heath examples (recommended):** Cialdini's seven principles (reciprocity, commitment/consistency, social proof, liking, authority, scarcity, unity) are `PUBLISHED_REFERENCE` (L3) with 7000+ citations and 40 years of replication. Weltman's voice + examples make it dinner-party-friendly. Heath brothers' SUCCESS framework adds the *practitioner application* layer.
- **C — Pure academia (Cialdini + ELM + framing literature alone):** Highest source quality but loses the "Seducing Strangers" hook the user actually asked for.

**Going with B.** The compound shape gives us the rigour of academic spine with Weltman as the recurring voice in Teaching Approach + Edge Cases. The Course Ref's *Sources Cited* names both, with Cialdini as primary.

## 4. Structure-match check

**Partial match (needs translation, not severe).**

- **Source-led shape:** Cialdini's seven principles as discrete chapters (Reciprocity / Commitment / Social Proof / Liking / Authority / Scarcity / Unity). Each principle has a "when it works / when it backfires / how it's misused" structure.
- **Audience-led shape:** "How do I sell better without becoming a sleaze?" / "How do those clever ad campaigns actually work on me?"

The principles map cleanly to the audience question — the audience wants tools, the literature provides tools. Translation tax is mostly tone: academic Cialdini reads dry; Weltman's voice gives it the "Mad Men" energy the audience came for. Solved by using Weltman's *examples* and *framing* throughout the Course Reference Teaching Approach, with Cialdini as the LO/recall spine.

## 5. Bottom-up validation — 3 sampled LOs

### LO sample 1: `pers.social_proof`

- **Definition:** People look to others' behaviour to decide what's right — particularly under uncertainty, when others are similar, and when there's evidence of voluntary action (Cialdini 2021, ch. 4). Mechanism: information ("if everyone's doing it, it must be valid") + social validation ("not doing it = standing out").
- **Recall items (HFF-authored Socratic probes, not reproductions):**
  1. "If a restaurant is empty at 8pm on a Friday night, what's your gut reaction? Why?" → Social proof in reverse: emptiness = signal not to enter. The principle works in both directions.
  2. "What's a famous case where 'tell people most others do the right thing' backfired?" → The Petrified Forest study (Cialdini et al. 2006): the sign saying "many past visitors have removed wood" *increased* theft because it normalised the behaviour. Descriptive vs injunctive norm collision.
  3. "When is social proof weakest as a persuasive tool?" → When the audience is high-elaboration / motivated (Petty & Cacioppo 1986 — peripheral cues lose power under central processing) and when the audience perceives the "many others" as dissimilar to themselves.
- **Misconception probe:** "Adding '10,000 customers can't be wrong' to your homepage always helps, right?" → Not if the customers shown are dissimilar to the visitor, or if the visitor is in high-elaboration mode (B2B enterprise buyer doing a deep evaluation). Worse: if "10,000 customers" reads as commodity rather than premium, you can move the brand the wrong way.

| Test | Result |
|---|---|
| Citable | ✅ Cialdini 2021 ch. 4 + Cialdini et al. 2006 + Petty & Cacioppo 1986 |
| Discriminative | ✅ Probes the principle, not adjacent ones |
| Voice-deliverable | ✅ Restaurant scenario is universal |
| Misconception-targeted | ✅ "More is always better" + the boomerang case both documented |

### LO sample 2: `pers.scarcity_loss`

- **Definition:** People place more value on things that are (or appear to be) scarce. Mechanism: scarcity signals quality + competition + loss aversion (Cialdini 2021, ch. 7; Kahneman & Tversky 1979). Losses feel ~2× as bad as equivalent gains feel good — so "you'll lose X" outpulls "you'll gain X."
- **Recall items:**
  1. "Why does 'only 3 left at this price' work harder than '70% off'?" → Scarcity activates loss aversion (Kahneman/Tversky); 70% off activates a gain frame which is weaker. Same economic message, asymmetric pull.
  2. "When does scarcity backfire?" → When the scarcity is obviously fake (countdown timer that resets), when the audience is sophisticated (B2B buyers, repeat customers), or when scarcity collides with the brand's "always available" promise.
  3. "Loss frame vs scarcity — same thing or different?" → Adjacent. Scarcity is *about the item* (it might run out); loss aversion is *about the buyer* (you might lose access). Both lean on the same underlying asymmetry but the frame differs.
- **Misconception probe:** "If scarcity works, more scarcity must work better — start every email with FINAL CHANCE, right?" → No. Frequent fake scarcity destroys trust permanently. The principle has a *credibility budget*; once that budget is gone, scarcity reads as manipulation and the entire brand suffers.

| Test | Result |
|---|---|
| Citable | ✅ Cialdini 2021 ch. 7 + Kahneman & Tversky 1979 |
| Discriminative | ✅ Pulls apart scarcity from loss frame |
| Voice-deliverable | ✅ Email/checkout examples are universal |
| Misconception-targeted | ✅ Fake-scarcity-fatigue is documented in CRM industry studies |

### LO sample 3: `pers.ethics_dual_use`

- **Definition:** The persuasion principles in this course can be used ethically (revealing existing value, helping good decisions) or manipulatively (manufacturing pressure, exploiting cognitive shortcuts to override the audience's interest). The line is intent + truthfulness: ethical persuasion *amplifies* what is true; manipulation *manufactures* what is not.
- **Recall items:**
  1. "Name the difference between 'real social proof' and 'manufactured social proof.'" → Real: actual customers' actual choices being reflected. Manufactured: invented testimonials, paid fake reviews, dummy social-media accounts. The principle works either way on the *audience*; the line is what's true.
  2. "Why does Cialdini explicitly object to teaching these principles for manipulative use?" → Cialdini frames the principles as *describing how humans work*. Manipulation is using that knowledge against the audience's interest. He argues practitioners have a professional responsibility to use the principles consistent with truth — and notes the long-term cost of misuse (trust erosion, brand decay, regulation).
  3. "Suppose your boss tells you to add fake scarcity to a checkout flow. What's the response?" → A range — refuse, push back with evidence on long-term damage, suggest A/B testing real-scarcity alternatives, document the request. Not a single right answer; this LO is about *seeing the choice* rather than dismissing it as inevitable.
- **Misconception probe:** "Persuasion is morally neutral — it's just a tool, what you do with it is up to you." → Partial. The principles describe mechanisms; the *application* is morally loaded. "Just a tool" is the language people use to disclaim responsibility for choices they could make differently. The mechanism is neutral; the choice to deploy it dishonestly is not.

| Test | Result |
|---|---|
| Citable | ✅ Cialdini 2021 epilogue + Pratkanis & Aronson (2001) *Age of Propaganda* |
| Discriminative | ✅ The only LO whose probe IS the misconception — that's by design |
| Voice-deliverable | ✅ |
| Misconception-targeted | ✅ "Just a tool" framing is the central rationalisation the field documents |

**Conclusion:** Bottom-up validation passes. The ethics LO is the linchpin that prevents the course from becoming a manipulation manual — it's not bolted on, it's threaded through.

## 6. Top-down validation

**Course outcome:** *"After 5 calls (~35 min), the learner can (a) name and recognise the seven Cialdini persuasion principles in real ads/pitches/sales conversations, (b) explain when each principle works and when it backfires, (c) distinguish ethical persuasion from manipulation, and (d) catch the three most common misuses they'll encounter (fake scarcity, manufactured social proof, manipulative reciprocity)."*

LO ladder check:
- 7 principle LOs (one per Cialdini principle) → covers (a)
- 7 "when it works / when it backfires" LOs (one per principle) → covers (b) — could compress to ~3-4 if budget tight
- 1 ethics LO + 1 misconception roundup LO → covers (c) + (d)
- 1 meta-LO (intro to persuasion, Weltman's framing, central vs peripheral routes)

= ~16 LOs total. Above EMA-visibility floor.

## 7. Failure-mode sweep

**Codifiable checks:**

1. **Source authority** — Weltman alone would be L2 (insufficient). Compound source (Cialdini L3 + Weltman L2 + Heath L2 + ELM L3 + Kahneman/Tversky L3) → PUBLISHED_REFERENCE ceiling for the course. **PASS.**
2. **Voice/visual mismatch** — Persuasion is conversational by nature. **PASS.**
3. **EMA mastery invisibility** — 16 LOs across 5 calls, default half-life nudge to 4 days. **PASS** with the same demo-half-life override as Big Five.
4. **Teaching profile compatibility** — `recall-led` for the principle naming layer + `discussion-led` overrides for the "is this ethical?" reflection layer. Combine via `teachingOverrides` as text instructions in Course Reference Teaching Approach (overrides aren't Wizard-settable per #963 review). **PASS.**
5. **`INSTRUCTION_CATEGORIES` collision** — All LOs learner-visible. **PASS.**

**Judgment calls:**

6. **Pop-folklore creep** — Persuasion is saturated with bro-marketing TikTok content. Strategy: name the popular alternatives in Module 1 ("you've seen 'attention hacks' content — here's what the actual research says"), use them as misconception material. Cialdini himself is now popular enough that the *good* sources are accessible. **PASS with surfaced flag.**
7. **Demo audience tune-out** — Adults find persuasion immediately relevant. **PASS.**
8. **Misconception density** — VERY HIGH. Every Cialdini principle has documented near-misses, anti-patterns, and folk misuses. Perfect catch-and-correct surface. **PASS.**
9. **Cross-cultural validity** — Cialdini's principles replicate cross-culturally but with calibration. Authority operates differently in high-power-distance vs low-power-distance cultures (Hofstede). Reciprocity has different timing norms. **FLAG** — include a brief cross-cultural LO in the final module.
10. **🆕 Dual-use risk** *(new failure mode flagged by this brief)* — **HIGH.** This topic teaches mechanisms that can be applied ethically or manipulatively. Standard catalogue precedents (Big Five, Attachment, Sleep) don't have this property at the same intensity. **Mitigation, baked into the course design:** dedicated ethics LO (`pers.ethics_dual_use`) that's not optional, not at the end — threaded through every principle module via Edge Cases ("how does this principle get misused, and how do you spot it?"). The course teaches the *recognition* of manipulation alongside the principles, not as a separate ethics-bolted-on.

## 8. Demo arc (call-by-call)

- **Call 1 (Foundations + Module 1 — Why Persuasion Works):** Weltman's "attention → attraction → action" frame, Cialdini's seven-principle map, ELM's two routes (central vs peripheral), meta-LO on ethics-as-frame. **Wow:** "There are roughly seven levers human beings can be persuaded with. Forty years of research. They work on you whether you've heard of them or not — and once you can name them, you can spot them being pulled on you in real time."
- **Call 2 (Reciprocity + Social Proof):** Two principles, deep. **Wow ("it remembered me"):** `priorCallRecap` — "Last call you mentioned a sales pitch where you felt 'really seen' — let's pressure-test that. Was it reciprocity (something genuinely given before any ask), or was it manufactured rapport?"
- **Call 3 (Scarcity + Loss Framing — and the misuse catch):** Cialdini scarcity + Kahneman/Tversky loss. **Wow ("it caught my error"):** Learner mentions a recent purchase they made under a countdown timer. Tutor probes: "Did the timer reset when you closed the tab? Was the scarcity *true* or *manufactured*?" Most of the time, the answer reframes the purchase — visceral catch-and-correct moment.
- **Call 4 (Liking + Authority + Commitment):** Three of the more behavioural principles. Liking ties to Weltman's "make the unfamiliar familiar." Authority ties to Milgram-era research. Commitment ties to foot-in-the-door experiments. Cross-cultural calibration introduced here.
- **Call 5 (Unity + Ethics + Application):** Cialdini's seventh (and newest) principle, the ethics roundup, and the application practice — give the learner three real ad campaigns / sales emails to dissect on their own. **Dashboard wow:** Mastery graph 13/16 green. Tutor closes with: "You'll catch yourself being persuaded by these for the rest of your life. That's the gift — and the responsibility goes the other way too."

## Skills (for the Skills Framework block in the Course Reference)

The course measures three skills, all assessed continuously across calls (no formal pre/post test).

- **SKILL-01: Principle Recognition** — can the learner spot a Cialdini principle being deployed in a real ad, sales pitch, email, or conversation?
  - *Emerging:* names 1–2 principles when prompted; can't reliably tag them in real material.
  - *Developing:* names all 7 principles; tags 3–5 of them correctly in supplied examples; occasionally conflates social proof with liking, or scarcity with loss-framing.
  - *Secure:* tags all 7 reliably in real material, including subtle / mixed-principle cases (e.g. "this email is reciprocity *and* commitment"). Distinguishes near-cousins.

- **SKILL-02: Misuse Identification** — can the learner spot the manipulative variant of each principle (fake scarcity, manufactured social proof, manipulative reciprocity, etc.)?
  - *Emerging:* notices "this feels off" but can't name the manipulation pattern.
  - *Developing:* names common misuses (fake scarcity, fake reviews) but misses subtler ones (manipulative reciprocity dressed as generosity, fake authority via vague credentials).
  - *Secure:* names the misuse pattern for any of the 7 principles; explains *why* it crosses the line (manufacturing something untrue vs amplifying something true).

- **SKILL-03: Ethical Application** — can the learner apply a principle honestly to their own work / communication, with explicit reasoning?
  - *Emerging:* picks principles by gut; can't articulate why one fits the situation; doesn't notice the ethical dimension until prompted.
  - *Developing:* picks principles with stated reasoning; can identify when a principle would be inappropriate (high-elaboration audience, mismatched audience similarity, etc.).
  - *Secure:* selects principle + frame consciously, articulates the truth claim being amplified, anticipates how the audience would respond if the deployment were exposed.
