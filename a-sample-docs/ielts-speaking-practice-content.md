## §1 Sample answers

### 1.1 Part 1 (Familiar topics) — short answers, 30 seconds

**Q: Do you live in a house or an apartment?**

> "I live in a small flat in the city centre. It's only a one-bedroom place, but it suits me because I'm out a lot for work, and the location means I can walk pretty much everywhere — which I really value. The downside is the lack of outdoor space, so I'm always sneaking off to the park at weekends."

**Q: Do you enjoy cooking?**

> "Honestly, it depends on my mood. On weeknights I usually throw something quick together — pasta, a stir-fry, that kind of thing. But at weekends, when I've got more time, I really enjoy experimenting with recipes I've never tried. I find it almost meditative."

**Q: How do you usually travel to work or college?**

> "I cycle most days, weather permitting. It takes about twenty minutes door to door, and I'd rather start the day with some fresh air than be packed onto a crowded train. When it's pouring with rain, though, I'll cave in and take the bus."

### 1.2 Part 2 (Long turn) — 1–2 minute talk

**Cue card: Describe a place you'd like to visit.**

> "A place I've always wanted to visit is Kyoto, in Japan. It's a city I've read about for years, and what draws me to it is this strange combination of the very old and the very modern coexisting. You've got these centuries-old temples and traditional tea houses sitting alongside neon-lit shopping streets and high-speed trains.
>
> I'd love to go in spring, during cherry blossom season — apparently the whole city turns pink for about ten days, and people picnic under the trees. That image has always stayed with me. I'd want to spend at least a week there, walking the Philosopher's Path, visiting the bamboo grove at Arashiyama, and eating my way through the local food markets.
>
> I think what really pulls me towards Kyoto is the sense of pace it has. From what I've read and watched, it's a city that values slowness and ritual — tea ceremonies, garden design, the way meals are served. That feels like the opposite of how I live day to day, and I think it would be genuinely restorative to experience it firsthand."

### 1.3 Part 3 (Discussion) — analytical answer

**Q: Why do you think tourism is so important to many countries?**

> "I think there are a few reasons. The most obvious one is economic — for some countries, tourism is a major part of GDP, providing jobs in hotels, restaurants, transport, and so on. Without it, you'd see real hardship in those communities.
>
> But beyond the economics, there's a softer benefit too. Tourism encourages countries to preserve their cultural and natural heritage — historic buildings, traditional crafts, national parks — because there's a clear incentive to keep them in good shape. Without visitors, some of those things might be neglected.
>
> That said, it cuts both ways. Over-tourism is becoming a serious issue in places like Venice or Barcelona, where the sheer volume of visitors is degrading the very things people came to see. So I'd say tourism is genuinely important, but it needs to be managed carefully — quality over quantity."

## §3 Practice cue cards

Use each card as a 1–2 minute monologue with 1 minute prep.

### Card A — Describe a teacher who influenced you

You should say:
- who the teacher was
- what subject they taught
- what made their style memorable
- and explain how they influenced the way you learn now.

### Card B — Describe a meal you'll always remember

You should say:
- where and when it was
- who you were with
- what you ate
- and explain why it stuck in your memory.

### Card C — Describe a piece of advice you ignored at the time

You should say:
- who gave you the advice
- what it was about
- why you didn't take it
- and explain what you think about it now.

### Card D — Describe an app or website you use almost daily

You should say:
- what it is
- when you first started using it
- how you use it now
- and explain what you'd do without it.

### Card E — Describe a moment you felt completely out of your depth

You should say:
- when and where this happened
- what was going on
- how you reacted
- and explain what you took away from it.

### Card F — Describe a routine that has stayed the same for you over the years

You should say:
- what the routine is
- when in the day you do it
- how it began
- and explain why you've kept it going.

## §4 Common collocations + phrasal verbs

### 4.1 Time + frequency
- *now and again, every so often, on the odd occasion* — for "sometimes"
- *day in, day out / round the clock / 24/7* — for "constantly"
- *as a rule / by and large / for the most part* — for "usually"

### 4.2 Opinion softeners (Part 3)
- "I'd say…" / "I suppose…" / "From what I gather…"
- "It depends on…" / "There's an argument that…"
- "I'm not entirely sure, but my hunch is…"

### 4.3 Strong agreement / disagreement
- *Couldn't agree more / spot on / hits the nail on the head*
- *Beg to differ / can't quite go along with that / not so sure*

### 4.4 Phrasal verbs for daily routine
- *get up, get on with, wind down, doze off, drop off, lie in*
- *catch up on, run out of (time), mess about, settle in, hang around*

### 4.5 Phrasal verbs for change
- *take up (a hobby), give up, cut down on, branch out, put off, knuckle down*

### 4.6 Phrasal verbs for relationships
- *get on with, fall out with, make up, drift apart, look up to, take after*

## §5 Pronunciation drills

### 5.1 Connected speech — linking + elision

Read aloud, slowly first, then at conversational pace. The marks show where words flow into one another (•) and where sounds drop (~).

> *I'• always thought tha~• people who~• grow up in~• cities and~• those who grow up in~• the country end~• up with quite different perspectives on~• what's important.*

> *To be~• honest, it'• not something I'• ever really thought aboot until quite recently — bu• now tha~• I'• thinking aboot it, I'• really not sure.*

### 5.2 Sentence stress — content vs function words

Stress only the **bold** words; let the rest run lightly.

> "I'**don't** **think** it's a **simple** **answer**, **really**. There are **two** **sides** to it."

> "**Most** **people** I **know** have **changed** **how** they **work** in the **past** **five** **years**."

### 5.3 Schwa drill (the relaxed "uh" sound)

Underlined letters reduce to /ə/. Read until it feels natural, not forced.

- The c**o**mp**a**ny **a**bout an **a**dventur**ous** w**o**m**a**n…
- A pers**o**n**a**l mem**o**ry fr**o**m my child**oo**d…
- A m**o**ment th**a**t comple**te**ly ch**a**nged my pe**r**spect**i**ve…

### 5.4 Minimal pairs — common confusions

Practise pairs aloud, then in sentences.

- *ship / sheep, bit / beat, fit / feet*  →  "I sat on the **ship** as the **sheep** wandered onto the **beat**en path."
- *thin / fin, three / free, math / map*  →  "**Three free** lessons should be enough to **think** about it."
- *very / berry, vest / best, vote / boat*  →  "The **best berry vest vote** went to the **boat** owner."

### 5.5 Long-form fluency drill

Read straight through aiming for 12–15 seconds per sentence, no pauses mid-sentence.

> *When I think about the things that have shaped me most over the past decade, what comes up first isn't the big achievements or the obvious milestones — it's actually the much smaller, daily decisions that, at the time, didn't feel like decisions at all.*

> *Looking back now, I can see how a quiet choice to keep a journal, or to take a walk every evening, or to call my parents on Sunday evenings — none of which felt significant in the moment — has ended up shaping the rhythm of my life in ways I never anticipated.*
