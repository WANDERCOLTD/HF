# IELTS Speaking — Assessor Rubric

This document is the rubric the scorer applies when assessing learner
performance on IELTS Speaking modules. It is **tutor-only / assessor-only**
content — never spoken to the learner verbatim. The tutor translates the
descriptors into plain-language feedback ("your fluency is closer to Band 6
than Band 7 because..."), referencing the specific behaviours below as
evidence.

Four learning outcomes (LOs) — one per official IELTS Speaking criterion.
Each LO covers Band 5 + Band 6 + Band 7 descriptors. Band 4 and Band 8+ are
acknowledged at the edges but not detailed here; the target band range for
this course is 5.0–7.5.

The scorer reads a transcript turn and assigns a band per criterion based on
the **best fit** across the descriptors, not a strict match. Bands can be
half-bands (5.5, 6.5, 7.5) where the evidence sits between two descriptors.

---

## LO: Fluency & Coherence

**Criterion:** the learner speaks at length without effortful hesitation, with logical sequencing of ideas and a range of cohesive devices.

**What "evidence" looks like:** average length of answer in seconds, number and type of hesitations (language-search vs idea-search), variety of connectives used, presence or absence of self-correction, ability to extend an answer beyond a single sentence.

**Band 5:** Usually maintains flow of speech but uses repetition, self-correction, and slow speech to keep going. Overuses certain connectives ("and", "then", "but") and discourse markers ("you know", "I think", "actually"). Produces simple speech fluently but more complex communication causes fluency problems. Long pauses while searching for words. Answers often a single sentence; struggles to extend.

**Band 6:** Willing to speak at length, though may lose coherence at times due to occasional repetition, self-correction, or hesitation. Uses a range of connectives and discourse markers but not always with clear effect (e.g. uses "however" where "moreover" would fit better). Answers reach 2–3 sentences; extensions present but sometimes feel forced or off-topic. Hesitations are mixed: some language-search, some idea-search.

**Band 7:** Speaks at length without noticeable effort or loss of coherence. May demonstrate language-related hesitation at times, or some repetition and/or self-correction. Uses a range of connectives and discourse markers with some flexibility. Hesitations are primarily language-related (searching for the right word) not content-related (searching for an idea). Answers naturally extend with relevant detail, examples, or qualifying clauses.

**Crossing into Band 7 typically requires:** sustained answers of 25+ seconds in Part 1, a 90+ second Part 2 long turn that doesn't trail off, and 30+ second Part 3 answers with at least one cohesive device used appropriately ("having said that", "to put it another way", "the other side of the coin is").

## LO: Lexical Resource

**Criterion:** the learner uses vocabulary precisely, with range, awareness of collocation, and some less common or idiomatic items appropriate to the topic.

**What "evidence" looks like:** topic-specific vocabulary present or absent, number of repetitions of high-frequency words ("good", "nice", "interesting", "important"), accurate collocation, presence of paraphrase when a word can't be retrieved, use of any less-common or idiomatic items, style appropriateness (formal vs informal register).

**Band 5:** Manages to talk about familiar and unfamiliar topics but uses vocabulary with limited flexibility. Attempts to use paraphrase but with mixed success. Relies on high-frequency words ("good", "nice", "important"). Word choice errors occasionally impede meaning. Some collocations correct, others wrong ("make a homework", "make a decision"). Limited topic-specific vocabulary — when asked about technology, talks about "computers" and "phones" without reaching for "device", "platform", "application", "feature".

**Band 6:** Has a wide enough vocabulary to discuss topics at length and make meaning clear in spite of inappropriacies. Generally paraphrases successfully when the precise word doesn't come. Topic-specific vocabulary appears but is sometimes generic ("the environment is bad" rather than "environmental degradation is severe"). Collocations are mostly correct; occasional lapses ("do a mistake" instead of "make a mistake"). May overreach with ambitious vocabulary that doesn't quite fit context.

**Band 7:** Uses vocabulary resource flexibly to discuss a variety of topics. Uses some less common and idiomatic vocabulary and shows awareness of style and collocation, with some inappropriate choices. Uses paraphrase effectively when a precise word doesn't come. Collocations are mostly accurate ("a steep learning curve", "to put down roots", "to take after my mother"). Hedges appropriately ("I'd say…", "in some ways…", "broadly speaking…"). Reaches for topic-specific vocabulary without prompting.

**Crossing into Band 7 typically requires:** at least 2–3 instances of less-common vocabulary or idiomatic phrases per minute of speech, accurate use of collocation, and the ability to paraphrase around a missing word ("I can't think of the exact word, but it's the kind of thing where…") rather than freezing or substituting a wildly wrong one.

## LO: Grammatical Range & Accuracy

**Criterion:** the learner uses a variety of sentence structures (simple, compound, complex, conditional, hypothetical) with controlled accuracy.

**What "evidence" looks like:** ratio of simple to complex sentences, accuracy of verb tense (especially mixed past/present/conditional), accurate use of articles (a/the), presence of subordinate clauses, conditionals, hypotheticals, and reported speech, error density in complex sentences specifically.

**Band 5:** Produces basic sentence forms with reasonable accuracy. Uses a limited range of more complex structures, usually with errors that may cause some comprehension problems. Most output is simple sentences ("I live in a flat. The flat is small. I like it."). Attempts at complex structures (conditionals, relative clauses) break down. Tense errors are common, especially mid-sentence tense-switching. Article errors are frequent ("I went to the school" / "I bought car").

**Band 6:** Uses a mix of simple and complex structures, but with limited flexibility. May make frequent mistakes with complex structures, though these rarely cause comprehension problems. Conditionals attempted but often imperfect ("If I will go..."). Relative clauses present but sometimes awkward. Tense control is mostly accurate within a sentence but may slip across a longer answer. Articles mostly accurate with occasional lapses.

**Band 7:** Uses a range of complex structures with some flexibility. Frequently produces error-free sentences, though some grammatical mistakes persist. Conditionals are mostly accurate, including second and third conditional ("If I had more time, I would..."). Relative clauses flow naturally ("...the kind of place where you can..."). Tense control is sustained across long turns. Errors are noticeable but rare and do not impede communication. Modal verbs used flexibly ("might have", "could have been", "should be").

**Crossing into Band 7 typically requires:** at least one accurate complex structure (conditional, relative clause, or hypothetical) every 2–3 sentences, sustained tense control across the Part 2 long turn, and a noticeable reduction in article errors compared with Band 6.

## LO: Pronunciation

**Criterion:** the learner produces intelligible speech with effective use of stress, rhythm, intonation, individual sounds, and connected-speech features (linking, elision, schwa reduction).

**What "evidence" looks like:** intelligibility (can the listener understand without strain?), sentence stress on content words, word stress on individual lexical items (especially multi-syllable words: "particularly", "advertisement", "comfortable"), linking between words, schwa reduction in unstressed syllables, intonation patterns (rising for questions, falling for statements), individual sound accuracy for sounds known to be hard (/θ/ /ð/ /v/ /w/ /r/ /l/ depending on L1).

**Important:** L1 accent alone is NOT penalised. The descriptors assess
intelligibility and use of pronunciation features, not closeness to a
native-speaker accent. A learner with a strong but clear accent can score
Band 7+ on Pronunciation.

**Band 5:** Shows all the positive features of Band 4 and some, but not all, of the positive features of Band 6. Uses a limited range of pronunciation features. Attempts to use sentence stress, rhythm, and intonation but control is uneven. Mispronounces individual sounds in ways that affect intelligibility some of the time. Listener has to concentrate to follow; some words are unclear.

**Band 6:** Uses a range of pronunciation features with mixed control. Shows some effective use of features (sentence stress in places, some linking, some schwa) but this is not sustained. Can generally be understood throughout, though mispronunciation of individual words or sounds reduces clarity at times. Word stress sometimes falls on the wrong syllable in less-common lexical items.

**Band 7:** Shows all the positive features of Band 6 and some, but not all, of the positive features of Band 8. Uses a range of pronunciation features flexibly. Sustains use of features (stress, rhythm, linking, schwa) with only occasional lapses. Is easy to understand throughout; L1 accent has minimal effect on intelligibility. Word stress is accurate even on multi-syllable lexical items. Connected speech flows ("I'd've", "should've", "going-to-be" linking).

**Crossing into Band 7 typically requires:** consistent intelligibility (the listener never has to ask for repetition), accurate sentence stress on content words, evident use of at least 2–3 connected-speech features (linking, schwa, weak forms), and accurate word stress on the multi-syllable vocabulary the learner reaches for.

## Scoring Procedure (for the scorer, not the learner)

1. Read the full transcript of the module before scoring.
2. For each of the four criteria, identify 2–3 specific pieces of evidence from the transcript (verbatim quotes are best).
3. Match the evidence pattern to the **best-fit** band descriptor above. Allow half-bands where the evidence sits between two descriptors.
4. Record the band + the verbatim evidence in the learner model.
5. In tutor mode, translate the band into plain language for the learner ("your fluency is closer to Band 6.5 than Band 7 because..."). Do not say the number unless the learner explicitly asks. In examiner mode (full mock), deliver the band number per criterion at the end.
6. If two criteria conflict (e.g. ambitious vocabulary but high error density), note the trade-off explicitly: "Your reach for less-common vocabulary lifts Lexical Resource toward Band 7, but the same reach is pulling Grammatical Range down — the complex structures are breaking under the load."
