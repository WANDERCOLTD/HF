# IELTS Speaking — Model Answers (Band 5 vs Band 7)

Paired exemplars for IELTS Speaking practice. Each block contains the same
question answered at two band levels, plus a one-line teacher annotation
that names the concrete linguistic features the Band 7 displays.

The tutor surfaces these **on request** — when the learner asks "what does
good look like?" — and reads the matching paired exemplar aloud at
conversational pace. The point is for the learner to produce language
first, hear the model second, then re-attempt with the modelled features
in mind.

Tags + `question_id` + `module` lines are formatted consistently so the
extractor can pull them as searchable metadata.

Coverage:
- §1 Part 1 — 6 questions across 3 topic modules (Home, Hobbies, Work/Study)
- §2 Part 2 — 3 cue cards (Person, Event, Place)
- §3 Part 3 — 6 questions across 3 discussion themes (Society, Technology, Education)
- §4 Skill drill micro-examples — 4 criteria × 2 tiers

## §1 Part 1 — Familiar topics (30-second answers)

### Q: Do you live in a house or an apartment?

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: p1_home_q1 module: p1_home
>
> "I live in a small flat in the city centre. It's only a one-bedroom place, but it suits me because I'm out a lot for work, and the location means I can walk pretty much everywhere — which I really value. The downside is the lack of outdoor space, so I'm always sneaking off to the park at weekends."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: p1_home_q1 module: p1_home
>
> "I live in apartment. It is in city. It is small but is good for me. I like it because is near to my work. Sometimes I want garden but I don't have. So I go to park."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** extends the answer with a trade-off ("it suits me because… The downside is…"), uses precise collocations ("city centre", "outdoor space", "sneaking off"), and varies sentence structure with a parenthetical clause ("— which I really value"). The Band 5 is grammatically simple (article-dropping, repeated "is") and lacks topic-specific vocabulary.

---

### Q: Tell me about the area where you grew up.

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: p1_home_q2 module: p1_home
>
> "I grew up in a fairly sleepy town on the outskirts of a bigger city — the kind of place where everyone knew everyone, for better or worse. As a kid I found it stifling, but looking back I can see the appeal of that tight-knit community. There were some downsides, mind you — not much to do, and you couldn't really do anything without word getting around."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: p1_home_q2 module: p1_home
>
> "I grew up in small town. The town is not big. Everyone know each other. It was okay. There was not much things to do but it was nice. Now I live in big city, it is different."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** uses idiomatic phrases ("sleepy town", "tight-knit community", "word getting around"), hedges naturally ("for better or worse", "mind you"), and shifts perspective across time ("As a kid… looking back…"). The Band 5 sticks to simple present and there-is/there-was, with no idiomatic items.

### Q: Do you have any hobbies?

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: p1_hobbies_q1 module: p1_hobbies
>
> "I've recently taken up bouldering, actually — it's a long-standing interest I'd never quite got around to. What I love about it is that it's both physical and a bit of a puzzle: you've got to read the route before you climb it. It's a brilliant way to switch off after work because you literally cannot think about anything else when you're on the wall."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: p1_hobbies_q1 module: p1_hobbies
>
> "Yes, I have hobby. I like to climb. I climb in the gym, not outside. It is good exercise and I like it. I do it two times in the week with my friend."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** uses a phrasal verb ("taken up"), an idiomatic collocation ("long-standing interest"), a relative clause ("I'd never quite got around to"), and a vivid figurative phrase ("switch off… cannot think about anything else when you're on the wall"). The Band 5 is on-topic but stays in present simple with high-frequency vocabulary throughout.

### Q: Do you enjoy cooking?

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: p1_hobbies_q2 module: p1_hobbies
>
> "Honestly, it depends on my mood. On weeknights I usually throw something quick together — pasta, a stir-fry, that kind of thing. But at weekends, when I've got more time, I really enjoy experimenting with recipes I've never tried. I find it almost meditative."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: p1_hobbies_q2 module: p1_hobbies
>
> "Yes, I cook. I cook in evening every day. I make easy food, like pasta or rice. I am not good cook but it is okay. Sometime I cook with my family on weekend."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** hedges ("Honestly, it depends on my mood"), uses an informal collocation ("throw something quick together"), names specific examples casually ("pasta, a stir-fry"), and finishes with an unexpected adjective ("almost meditative"). The Band 5 is on-topic but uncontracted, with frequency errors ("Sometime") and missing articles.

### Q: What kind of work do you do, or what do you study?

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: p1_work_study_q1 module: p1_work_study
>
> "I work in marketing for a software company — I'm responsible for the brand side of things, so anything from campaign messaging to the look and feel of the product. It's a deadline-driven role, which I both love and occasionally curse, but on balance I find it genuinely creative most days."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: p1_work_study_q1 module: p1_work_study
>
> "I work in marketing. I work for software company. My job is to make adverts and to think of ideas. It is busy job but I like it. I work with team of five people."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** unpacks the role with concrete scope ("anything from campaign messaging to the look and feel"), uses a topic-specific collocation ("deadline-driven role"), and adds a balanced evaluation ("which I both love and occasionally curse… on balance"). The Band 5 lists facts in simple sentences without scoping or evaluating.

### Q: How do you usually travel to work or college?

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: p1_work_study_q2 module: p1_work_study
>
> "I cycle most days, weather permitting. It takes about twenty minutes door to door, and I'd rather start the day with some fresh air than be packed onto a crowded train. When it's pouring with rain, though, I'll cave in and take the bus."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: p1_work_study_q2 module: p1_work_study
>
> "I go to work by bike. It is twenty minutes. I like to ride bike, it is good for health. When the weather is bad, I take bus, but I don't like the bus because is too crowded."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** uses an absolute construction ("weather permitting"), an idiomatic comparison phrase ("door to door"), a 'd rather + bare infinitive structure, and an informal phrasal verb ("cave in"). The Band 5 is grammatically clear but stays in simple sentences with no idiomatic items.

## §2 Part 2 — Long turn (1.5–2 minute cue cards)

### Cue card: Describe a person who has influenced you.

You should say:
- who the person is
- how you know them
- what they are like
- and explain how they have influenced you.

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: p2_describe_person_q1 module: p2_describe_person
>
> "The person who comes to mind straight away is my old English teacher from secondary school — a man called Mr Hennessy. He was probably in his late fifties when he taught me, so I'd have been about fifteen.
>
> He wasn't the sort of teacher who stood at the front and lectured. He used to wander around the classroom, perch on the edge of someone's desk, and just ask questions — most of them genuinely curious questions, not the kind teachers ask when they already know the answer. He had a sort of quiet authority that meant nobody ever messed around in his class, but it wasn't because we were scared of him; it was because we didn't want to disappoint him.
>
> What's stayed with me is the way he treated us as if our opinions actually mattered. We were fifteen, we knew nothing, and he'd still listen to whatever half-formed argument we put to him as though it might be the thing that changed his mind. Looking back, I think that's what real teaching is — taking the other person seriously.
>
> The influence on me has been pretty deep, actually. I work in a job now where I have to listen to people for a living, and the way I do it — the questions I ask, the silences I leave — comes more or less directly from him. So I'd say he shaped me more than anyone outside my own family."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: p2_describe_person_q1 module: p2_describe_person
>
> "I want to talk about my English teacher. His name is Mr Hennessy. He was my teacher in school when I was fifteen years old. He is older man, maybe sixty years old now.
>
> He was a good teacher. He didn't shout. He asked many questions to the students. The students liked him because he was kind.
>
> I think he influenced me because he listened to students. Many teachers don't listen but he listened. He thought our ideas were important.
>
> Now I am working in job. I think about my teacher sometime. I try to listen to people in my work, like him. So this is the person I want to talk about. He was good influence for me."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** sustains a 1.5-minute long turn without trailing off, uses descriptive phrases instead of evaluative adjectives ("a sort of quiet authority", "half-formed argument", "the silences I leave"), and lands cleanly on a thematic conclusion ("he shaped me more than anyone outside my own family"). The Band 5 covers the same four bullet points but stays in present/past simple, repeats high-frequency verbs ("was", "didn't", "listen"), and has no figurative or idiomatic language.

### Cue card: Describe a memorable event in your life.

You should say:
- when and where it happened
- who was with you
- what happened
- and explain why it was memorable.

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: p2_describe_event_q1 module: p2_describe_event
>
> "The event that springs to mind is my graduation from university — which sounds like a fairly obvious choice, but bear with me, because it wasn't memorable for the reasons you'd expect.
>
> It was in the summer of about seven years ago, at the university itself, which is a place I have very mixed feelings about. My parents had flown over for it, which was a big deal because they hadn't seen me in person for nearly two years at that point, and my younger brother had taken the day off school and come up on the train.
>
> The ceremony itself was, frankly, a bit of an anticlimax — you sit for hours, you walk across a stage for about eight seconds, and then you sit for hours again. What I actually remember, though, is the moment afterwards, when the four of us were standing on the lawn in our slightly damp coats, and my dad — who never really says anything emotional — just shook my hand and said, 'Right, well done.' That was it. And somehow that was everything.
>
> The reason it stays with me is partly the contrast — all that pomp and ceremony, and then the bit that actually mattered was about three seconds long and almost wordless. It taught me something about my dad that I think I'd always known but never quite put into words. So it's a memory I come back to fairly often, even now."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: p2_describe_event_q1 module: p2_describe_event
>
> "I want to talk about my graduation. It was seven years ago, in the summer. It was at my university.
>
> My family came to the event. My mother, my father and my brother. They travelled to see me. It was important for me because they don't see me often.
>
> The ceremony was long. I sit for many hours. Then I walk on the stage and I take my certificate. After that, we go outside. We took some photos.
>
> It was a special day because my family was there. My father said 'Well done' to me. It was nice moment for me. I will remember this day for long time."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** structures the answer around a contrast ("which sounds like a fairly obvious choice, but bear with me"), uses connected-speech-friendly phrases ("a bit of an anticlimax", "about three seconds long and almost wordless"), and lands on a reflective conclusion. The Band 5 is on-topic, hits all four bullets, but mixes tenses inconsistently ("I sit", "I walk", past intended) and stays in short flat sentences.

---

### Cue card: Describe a place you'd like to visit.

You should say:
- where it is
- what you know about it
- when you'd like to go
- and explain why you'd like to visit.

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: p2_describe_place_q1 module: p2_describe_place
>
> "A place I've always wanted to visit is Kyoto, in Japan. It's a city I've read about for years, and what draws me to it is this strange combination of the very old and the very modern coexisting. You've got these centuries-old temples and traditional tea houses sitting alongside neon-lit shopping streets and high-speed trains.
>
> I'd love to go in spring, during cherry blossom season — apparently the whole city turns pink for about ten days, and people picnic under the trees. That image has always stayed with me. I'd want to spend at least a week there, walking the Philosopher's Path, visiting the bamboo grove at Arashiyama, and eating my way through the local food markets.
>
> I think what really pulls me towards Kyoto is the sense of pace it has. From what I've read and watched, it's a city that values slowness and ritual — tea ceremonies, garden design, the way meals are served. That feels like the opposite of how I live day to day, and I think it would be genuinely restorative to experience it firsthand."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: p2_describe_place_q1 module: p2_describe_place
>
> "I want to visit Kyoto in Japan. It is famous city in Japan. There are many temples and gardens. The food is also good there, I want to try.
>
> I want to go in the spring time because it has the cherry blossom. The cherry blossom is pink flower and it is very beautiful. Many tourist go to see this flower.
>
> I want to go for one week. I will visit temple and garden. I will eat the Japanese food.
>
> I want to visit Kyoto because is different from my country. My country is busy and noisy. Japan is more quiet, I think. It will be nice holiday for me."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** opens with a hook ("what draws me to it is this strange combination of the very old and the very modern coexisting"), uses descriptive juxtaposition ("centuries-old temples… alongside neon-lit shopping streets"), and finishes with an abstract reflection ("a city that values slowness and ritual… genuinely restorative"). The Band 5 covers the same four bullets but lists facts without descriptive layering, and uses high-frequency adjectives ("famous", "beautiful", "good").

## §3 Part 3 — Discussion (30–45 second analytical answers)

### Q: Why do you think some communities feel more connected than others?

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: p3_society_change_q1 module: p3_society_change
>
> "I'd say it's down to a couple of things, really. The most obvious one is size — smaller, more stable communities tend to have stronger bonds simply because people see each other repeatedly over years, which builds trust. The other factor, I think, is shared institutions: a local school, a corner shop, a place of worship, even a pub. Where those exist, you get repeated low-stakes contact, which is the soil that real community grows in. Big cities can replicate this at the neighbourhood level, but it's harder."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: p3_society_change_q1 module: p3_society_change
>
> "I think some communities are more connected because people know each other. In small village, everyone know each other but in big city, you don't know your neighbour. So the small place is more connected. Also if people stay in same place for many years, they become more like family. In big city people move a lot, so it is harder."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** signposts the answer ("I'd say it's down to a couple of things"), names abstract drivers ("shared institutions", "repeated low-stakes contact"), uses a vivid metaphor ("the soil that real community grows in"), and acknowledges a nuance ("Big cities can replicate this… but it's harder"). The Band 5 reaches a similar conclusion but in concrete simple sentences without abstraction.

---

### Q: Do you think traditions are disappearing in modern society?

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: p3_society_change_q2 module: p3_society_change
>
> "Some are, yes — but I think the picture is more nuanced than 'traditions are dying out'. What I see is that traditions are being reshaped rather than lost. Take family meals, for example: in my grandparents' day that meant everyone sat down at six and ate the same food. Now it might mean a video call with a parent who lives in a different country. The form has changed, but the underlying ritual — pausing to reconnect — is arguably stronger than ever. So I'd push back gently on the idea that we're losing traditions; I think we're updating them."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: p3_society_change_q2 module: p3_society_change
>
> "Yes, I think some traditions are disappearing. Young people don't follow traditions like old people did. For example, in my country, families used to eat dinner together every day. Now, people are busy and they don't do this. Also, people don't go to church like before. So I think traditions are slowly disappearing. It is sad but it is the modern life."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** takes a position then complicates it ("Some are, yes — but I think the picture is more nuanced"), uses a concrete then-vs-now example to make the point, and lands on a reframe ("we're not losing traditions, we're updating them"). The Band 5 takes a position and supports it but doesn't complicate or qualify; vocabulary stays at high-frequency level.

### Q: How has technology changed the way people communicate?

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: p3_technology_q1 module: p3_technology
>
> "It's been a double-edged sword, hasn't it. On the positive side, technology has more or less abolished distance for people who can afford it — I can speak face-to-face with someone on the other side of the world for free, which would have seemed like science fiction thirty years ago. The flipside, though, is that the quality of communication has arguably suffered. We send more messages but have fewer real conversations. We're always reachable but rarely fully present. So while the volume of communication has exploded, the depth of it has thinned out in ways we haven't quite reckoned with yet."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: p3_technology_q1 module: p3_technology
>
> "Technology changed communication a lot. Now we can talk to people in any country with phone or computer. It is very easy and fast. We use WhatsApp and email and video call. Before, people wrote letters and it took long time. Now is much faster. Also we can see the person on video, not only the voice. So technology made communication better, in my opinion."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** opens with a discourse marker that flags evaluation ("It's been a double-edged sword, hasn't it"), uses a topic-specific phrase ("abolished distance"), names a trade-off explicitly ("the volume of communication has exploded, the depth of it has thinned"), and ends on a reflective note. The Band 5 lists facts and reaches a one-sided conclusion ("technology made communication better").

---

### Q: Do you think people rely too much on technology?

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: p3_technology_q2 module: p3_technology
>
> "I'd say we do, in certain domains — though I'm wary of the blanket claim. Where I think the over-reliance shows up most is in attention and memory: most of us have outsourced our recall of phone numbers, directions, and even appointments to our phones, and the muscle has atrophied. I noticed this myself the other day when my phone died and I genuinely couldn't remember my partner's number. That said, in other areas — work, learning, staying in touch — technology amplifies what we'd want to do anyway. So I'd land on: over-reliant in low-level cognitive tasks, but not in the bigger picture."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: p3_technology_q2 module: p3_technology
>
> "Yes, I think people use technology too much. People always look at the phone. They check messages many time in day. Also, people don't talk to each other in real life. They prefer to chat on phone. In restaurant or in bus, everyone is looking at phone. It is not good for health and for relationship. So yes, we use technology too much."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** qualifies the claim immediately ("in certain domains — though I'm wary of the blanket claim"), uses precise vocabulary ("outsourced our recall", "the muscle has atrophied"), brings in a personal anecdote to make the point concrete, and lands on a balanced answer rather than a one-sided one. The Band 5 takes a position and supports it with everyday observations in simple present.

### Q: How has education changed in the last 50 years?

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: p3_education_q1 module: p3_education
>
> "Quite dramatically, in a few different directions. The most obvious shift is the move from rote learning to a more student-centred approach — at least on paper; whether that's really happened in every classroom is another question. Class sizes have generally come down, and the relationship between teachers and students has become less formal, less hierarchical. The other big change is access: education has opened up to groups who were historically excluded — women in many countries, working-class kids, students with learning differences. That's the bigger story, I think, even more than the pedagogy."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: p3_education_q1 module: p3_education
>
> "Education changed a lot in 50 years. Before, students learn from book and teacher only. Now they use computer and internet. Also, before the teacher was very strict but now teachers are more friendly. There are also more students who go to university now, especially women. So education is much different now."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** flags the answer's structure ("Quite dramatically, in a few different directions"), inserts a hedge ("at least on paper; whether that's really happened in every classroom is another question"), uses register-appropriate vocabulary ("student-centred approach", "less hierarchical", "historically excluded"), and ranks the two changes ("That's the bigger story, even more than the pedagogy"). The Band 5 names similar changes but in concrete everyday vocabulary.

### Q: Do you think exams are the best way to measure learning?

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: p3_education_q2 module: p3_education
>
> "Not the best way, no — though they're convenient, which is partly why we keep using them. The strongest argument for exams is that they're standardised and comparable: a thousand students sit the same paper, and you can rank them. The weakness is that they reward a particular kind of thinking — fast, individual, under pressure — which has very little to do with how people actually use knowledge in life or in most jobs. I'd say the ideal is a mix: some standardised assessment for comparability, plus portfolio-style work that lets students demonstrate sustained, applied thinking. Relying on exams alone tells you which students can pass exams, which isn't quite the same thing as which students can think."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: p3_education_q2 module: p3_education
>
> "I don't think exams are best way to measure learning. Exams test if you can remember information for short time. But after exam, you forget it. So it doesn't show real learning. Also, some students are very nervous in exam and they cannot do their best. I think project work or homework is better way to see what student learn. Or maybe teacher should look at student work over the whole year, not only the exam."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** acknowledges the counter-position before disagreeing ("though they're convenient, which is partly why we keep using them"), uses parallel structure ("they reward a particular kind of thinking — fast, individual, under pressure"), proposes a synthesis rather than a binary answer, and lands on a memorable distinction ("which students can pass exams… isn't quite the same thing as which students can think"). The Band 5 takes a clear position, supports it with two reasons, but stays at sentence level rather than structural level.

## §4 Skill drill — micro-examples per criterion

These are short turns showing the difference between Band 5 and Band 7 on
**one criterion at a time**, with the other three criteria roughly equal
across the two answers. Used when the tutor wants to isolate a single
feature for the learner to hear and copy.

### Fluency & Coherence — extending an answer

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: drill_fluency_q1 module: drill_fluency
>
> "Yes, I do — I cycle most days, and I'd say it's become the part of my routine I'd miss most if I had to give it up. There's something about being outside first thing that sets up the rest of the day, even when the weather's awful."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: drill_fluency_q1 module: drill_fluency
>
> "Yes, I cycle. I cycle every day to work. I like cycling. It is good for the body and for the mind also."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** extends the same yes/no answer into three flowing clauses with a relative clause ("the part of my routine I'd miss most if I had to give it up") and an idea-extension at the end. The Band 5 contains four short sentences that each restart from a fresh subject, breaking the flow.

---

### Lexical Resource — reaching for the precise word

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: drill_lexical_q1 module: drill_lexical
>
> "He's quite a level-headed person — not one of those people who panics in a crisis. If anything, he's almost too unflappable; I sometimes wish he'd show a bit more reaction when things go wrong."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: drill_lexical_q1 module: drill_lexical
>
> "He is a calm person. He doesn't get nervous. He is always relaxed, even in difficult time. Sometimes I think he is too relaxed."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** reaches for less-common precise adjectives ("level-headed", "unflappable") instead of high-frequency ones ("calm", "relaxed"), and uses "if anything" as a discourse marker to flag a qualification. The Band 5 covers the same idea with everyday vocabulary throughout.

---

### Grammatical Range & Accuracy — using complex structures

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: drill_grammar_q1 module: drill_grammar
>
> "If I'd known how long it was going to take, I probably wouldn't have started — though looking back, I'm glad I did, because it's the kind of project that's changed how I think about my work."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: drill_grammar_q1 module: drill_grammar
>
> "It took a long time. I didn't know it will take so long. If I knew before, maybe I don't start. But now I am happy that I did this project. It changed my work."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** uses a third conditional accurately ("If I'd known… I wouldn't have started"), a relative clause ("the kind of project that's changed how I think"), and embeds a parenthetical reflection ("— though looking back, I'm glad I did"). The Band 5 attempts the conditional but the tense control breaks down ("If I knew… maybe I don't start").

---

### Pronunciation — sentence stress + connected speech

> **Band 7 — model answer**
> tags: [band-7] question_id: drill_pronunciation_q1 module: drill_pronunciation
>
> "*I'd've thought you'd've heard about that by now* — it's been all over the news for the last week or so. Particularly on the radio. They've been talking about almost nothing else."

> **Band 5 — weaker answer**
> tags: [band-5] question_id: drill_pronunciation_q1 module: drill_pronunciation
>
> "I think you have heard about that. It is in the news for one week. Especially on the radio. They talk about it many times."

**Why Band 7 is stronger:** uses connected-speech contractions naturally ("I'd've thought you'd've heard"), drops sentence stress onto content words ("*all* over the *news*", "*nothing* else"), and runs words together at natural pace. The Band 5 separates each word, uncontracted, with even stress across the sentence — fully intelligible but markedly less natural.
