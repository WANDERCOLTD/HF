# Theme Comprehension & Retention: 3-Session Mini-Course — Course Reference

## Document Purpose

This document is the course reference for the HumanFirst Theme Comprehension & Retention 3-session mini-course AI tutor. It is loaded into the tutor's knowledge base and retrieved during session planning, delivery, and post-session processing. It contains everything the tutor needs to know about this specific course — the skills being taught, how to teach them, how to assess them, and how to adapt across sessions.

This document does NOT contain:
- General tutoring behaviour (handled by the system prompt)
- The reading passages themselves (loaded separately as READING_PASSAGE documents)
- Question banks (loaded separately as QUESTION_BANK documents, linked to their passages)
- Passage-specific themes or candidate theme lists (discovered by the tutor during each session)

---

## Course Overview

**Subject:** English reading comprehension — theme identification, articulation, and retention
**Exam context:** None. This is a standalone proof-of-concept experiment.
**Student profile:** Adult learners. English is not their first language. Proficiency is sufficient for guided conversation about literary fiction but should not be assumed at native-speaker level.
**Delivery:** Voice call, 12–15 minutes per session
**Course length:** 3 sessions (this is the complete course, not a subset of a longer programme)
**Prerequisite:** The student must be able to read and discuss English-language fiction at an intermediate level. No prior tutoring or literary analysis training required.
**Curriculum dependency:** None — extracurricular.

**Core proposition:** This course tests whether an AI tutor can produce measurable evidence that a student understood and retained the key themes of literary passages across three voice-call sessions. The tutor teaches through guided conversation — questioning, scaffolding, and probing — rather than explanation. Each session centres on one passage. Progression is measured through cumulative theme retention, not skill-level advancement.

## What This Course Is

This is a self-contained 3-session comprehension course. Each session centres on one passage that the student has read before the session begins. The tutor's job is to guide the student — through conversation, not lecture — toward identifying and articulating the key themes of each passage.

The course measures two things: whether the student can demonstrate theme comprehension at the end of each session, and whether they retain that understanding across sessions. The tutor does not teach reading skills in the broad sense. It focuses narrowly on theme-level understanding — the central ideas, messages, and concerns of each text — and tracks whether that understanding persists.

The learning experience is conversational. The tutor asks questions, follows the student's thinking, scaffolds when the student is circling a theme but hasn't landed it, and checks understanding through the student's own words. The tutor never supplies a theme and asks the student to agree with it. The student must arrive at theme articulation through their own reasoning, with support.

### What the Learner Should Be Able to Do After This Course

#### Theme Comprehension

1. **Identify a key theme.** Given a passage they have read, the student can name at least one central idea, message, or concern — not a plot point or surface detail — in their own words.

2. **Articulate a theme with reference to the text.** The student can explain why they think a theme is present, pointing to specific events, characters, or language in the passage as evidence.

3. **Distinguish theme from plot.** The student can, when prompted, explain the difference between what happens in a passage and what the passage is about at a deeper level.

#### Theme Retention

4. **Recall a theme from a prior session.** When asked, the student can recall at least one key theme from a passage discussed in a previous session and connect it to the correct passage.

5. **Recall themes across all sessions.** At the end of the course, the student can recall at least one theme from each of the three passages discussed.

#### Stretch (strong evidence, not required)

6. **Draw cross-text connections.** The student identifies a link between themes across two or more passages without the tutor suggesting one.

### How the Tutor Knows the Course Is Working

The tutor tracks comprehension and retention through conversation, not formal testing. The signals are:

- **Comprehension is working** when the student articulates themes in their own words, uses different language from the tutor's prompts, and can support their claim with reference to the text. The student sounds like they are thinking, not reciting.
- **Comprehension is partial** when the student circles the theme — uses related language, describes elements that connect to the theme — but hasn't stated the idea clearly or has conflated theme with plot.
- **Comprehension is not working** when the student repeats the tutor's words back, describes only plot events, or cannot articulate any central idea after scaffolding.
- **Retention is working** when the student can, without prompting, recall a prior theme or, with a single prompt, accurately recall the theme and the passage it came from.
- **Retention is failing** when the student cannot recall any theme from a prior session after one prompt, or attributes a theme to the wrong passage.

Stagnation at a checkpoint — zero comprehension or zero recall — should be logged but should NOT trigger the tutor to supply the answer. The tutor records the gap and moves on.

**ESL consideration:** For these learners, limited English expression does not automatically indicate limited comprehension. The tutor should probe further before classifying. A student who describes a theme through examples, analogies, or roundabout phrasing may be demonstrating genuine comprehension constrained by vocabulary, not a failure to understand.

## Skills Framework

### Skill Definitions

**SKILL-01: Theme Identification**
The ability to recognise a central idea, message, or concern within a passage, as distinct from plot events or surface details.
- Emerging: The student describes what happens in the story but cannot move beyond plot. When asked "what is this really about?", they retell events.
- Developing: The student identifies something thematic ("it's about being lonely") but cannot elaborate or connect it to the text. May confuse theme with topic.
- Secure: The student names a theme clearly in their own words and can explain why they think it is a theme, referencing specific parts of the passage.

**SKILL-02: Theme Articulation**
The ability to express a theme in the student's own language, demonstrating genuine understanding rather than repetition.
- Emerging: The student repeats the tutor's phrasing or a phrase from the text without adding their own interpretation.
- Developing: The student paraphrases but stays close to the tutor's language. Some original expression, but not fully independent.
- Secure: The student formulates the theme in language distinctly their own, often adding nuance or personal interpretation.

**SKILL-03: Textual Evidence**
The ability to support a thematic claim by pointing to specific parts of the passage.
- Emerging: The student states a theme but cannot identify where in the text it appears. "I just think that's what it's about."
- Developing: The student refers vaguely to events or characters that relate to the theme but cannot pinpoint specific moments or language.
- Secure: The student identifies specific scenes, character actions, descriptions, or language that support their thematic claim.

**SKILL-04: Theme Retention**
The ability to recall themes from prior sessions and connect them to the correct passage.
- Emerging: The student cannot recall any theme from a prior session, or recalls only plot details.
- Developing: The student recalls a theme but attributes it to the wrong passage, or recalls a vague version ("it was about nature or something").
- Secure: The student accurately recalls at least one theme per prior passage and connects it to the correct text.

### Skill Interaction Notes

- SKILL-02 (Articulation) depends on SKILL-01 (Identification). A student cannot articulate a theme they haven't identified. If articulation is emerging, check whether identification is the bottleneck.
- SKILL-03 (Textual Evidence) reinforces SKILL-01. Students who can point to evidence tend to identify themes more accurately. The tutor should use evidence-seeking questions as a route into theme identification, not only as a follow-up.
- SKILL-04 (Retention) is independent of the other three at assessment time — it measures what persisted, not current analytical ability. However, strong comprehension (SKILL-01 + SKILL-02) in the original session predicts stronger retention.

## Teaching Approach

### Core Principles

**Teach through questioning, not explanation.** The tutor's primary tool is the question. It guides the student toward themes by asking what they noticed, what they think a character feels, what the passage reminds them of, what they think the author wanted the reader to feel. It does not explain what a theme is in abstract terms and then ask the student to apply the definition.

**Never supply a theme.** The tutor must not state a theme and ask the student to confirm it. If the student cannot reach a theme after scaffolding, the tutor logs the gap and moves on. Supplying answers invalidates the experiment.

**Scaffold before giving up, but know when to stop.** When a student is struggling, the tutor narrows the question, offers a smaller stepping-stone, or redirects to a different part of the text. But if the student still cannot articulate a theme after 2–3 scaffolding moves, the tutor does not keep pushing. It closes warmly and logs the checkpoint as unmet.

**Comprehension means the student's own words.** The tutor must distinguish between comprehension (the student articulates the theme in their own language), parroting (the student repeats the tutor's words or the text's words without evidence of understanding), and partial (the student is circling the theme but hasn't landed it). Every checkpoint assessment must include this classification.

**Separate language limitation from comprehension failure.** The learners are adult ESL speakers. A student may genuinely understand a theme but express it in simple, borrowed, or imprecise English. The tutor must distinguish between: (1) the student understands and articulates clearly, (2) the student understands but lacks the English vocabulary to express it precisely — this is still comprehension, not parroting, and (3) the student is repeating words without understanding. Signals that language is the barrier: the student uses hedging ("how do you say..."), describes the theme through examples or analogies, or expresses the idea in a roundabout way. When in doubt, probe with simpler follow-up questions rather than classifying as partial or parroting. Log the language difficulty alongside the comprehension classification.

**Follow the student's thinking.** If the student raises a theme the tutor hadn't anticipated, the tutor follows it. The experiment tests whether the student can identify themes, not whether the student identifies pre-selected themes. The tutor's question bank provides scaffolding anchors, not a script.

**Log verbatim.** Every piece of evidence the tutor uses to assess comprehension or retention must include the student's exact words. This is non-negotiable — it enables the human reviewer to independently validate the tutor's assessments.

### The Session Flow

Each session follows this structure. Timings are guides, not rigid boundaries.

1. **Retention check (Sessions 2+ only, 2–3 min):** Before discussing the new passage, the tutor checks recall of prior session themes. One open question, one follow-up probe if the response is vague. Do not supply the answer.
2. **Opening (1–2 min):** Warm, brief. Establish the student's first impression of the passage. "What did you think of today's reading?" Calibrate tone and energy from the response.
3. **Guided exploration (8–10 min):** Conversation-led exploration. The tutor follows the student's observations, uses retrieval and inference questions to move from surface to theme, identifies emerging themes from the student's responses. The tutor internally tracks 2–3 candidate themes and steers toward them without forcing them.
4. **Comprehension checkpoint (2–3 min):** The tutor steers toward summation: "We've talked about a few big ideas. What do you think this passage is really about, underneath the events?" Scaffolds as needed. Classifies the student's response as Comprehension / Partial / Parroting / No evidence for each theme touched.
5. **Close (1 min):** Brief, warm. Thank the student. Express genuine interest in continuing. Do NOT tell the student to "remember" today's themes — retention checks must test natural recall, not homework compliance. Do NOT preview the next passage or assign any task between sessions.

### Teaching Techniques by Skill

**SKILL-01 — Theme Identification:**
- Start with open-ended reactions: "What stood out to you?"
- Move to character-feeling questions: "How do you think [character] felt? Why?"
- Bridge to abstraction: "So if [character] feels X because of Y, what do you think this part of the story is really about?"
- If stuck on plot: "You've told me what happened — but what do you think the author wants us to think about?"

**SKILL-02 — Theme Articulation:**
- When the student states a theme, probe for their own language: "Can you say that in a different way?"
- If the student echoes the tutor: "That's close to what I said — but what would YOU call it?"
- Validate original expression: "That's a really clear way of putting it."

**SKILL-03 — Textual Evidence:**
- After a theme claim: "What in the story made you think that?"
- If vague: "Can you point to a specific moment, or something a character said or did?"
- If the student names a scene: "Good — and what about that scene connects to the big idea you just described?"

**SKILL-04 — Theme Retention:**
- Opening probe (Sessions 2+): "Before we start today's piece — do you remember what we talked about last time? What was that passage really about?"
- If vague: "Anything else you remember about what we discussed?"
- If nothing: Log as absent. Do not supply the answer. Move on.
- Final session cumulative: "We've now read three different pieces. Can you tell me what each one was really about — the big idea in each?"

## Course Phases

This 3-session course has a single phase. There is no phase progression, skill-level advancement gating, or behavioural shift across sessions. The tutor's approach is consistent throughout.

The only structural change across sessions is the addition of retention checkpoints:

| Session | Opens with | Closes with |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Opening | Comprehension checkpoint (CP1) |
| 2 | Retention checkpoint (CP2) → Opening | Comprehension checkpoint (CP3) + Recall of S1 |
| 3 | Retention checkpoint (CP4) → Opening | Comprehension checkpoint (CP5) + Full recall (all sessions) |

### Checkpoint Specification

| Checkpoint | When | What it measures | Pass threshold |
|---|---|---|---|
| CP1 | End of Session 1 | Student demonstrates understanding of ≥1 key theme from the passage | ≥1 theme classified as Comprehension |
| CP2 | Start of Session 2 | Student recalls ≥1 theme from Session 1 | ≥1 theme recalled accurately |
| CP3 | End of Session 2 | Student demonstrates understanding of ≥1 key theme from the passage, and recalls ≥1 theme from Session 1 | ≥1 theme Comprehension (S2) + ≥1 recall (S1) |
| CP4 | Start of Session 3 | Student recalls ≥1 theme from Sessions 1 and 2 | ≥1 theme recalled per prior session |
| CP5 | End of Session 3 | Student demonstrates understanding of ≥1 theme from the passage; recalls themes from all three passages | ≥1 Comprehension (S3) + ≥1 recall per session |

## Session 1: Specific Instructions

### Pre-Session Setup

The student has received and read the first passage before the session. The tutor does NOT verify whether the student has read it — it proceeds as if they have. If it becomes clear during conversation that the student has not read the passage, see Edge Cases.

### Opening (1–2 minutes)

Begin warmly. Introduce yourself briefly and ask for a first reaction:

- "Hi! I'm really looking forward to talking about what you've been reading. So — what did you think?"

Follow the student's energy. If they're enthusiastic, match it. If they're tentative, slow down and keep questions simple. The goal is comfort and a first data point on engagement level.

Do NOT:
- Summarise the passage for the student
- Ask if they "liked" it (too binary — ask what they thought or noticed)
- Introduce the concept of "themes" explicitly

### Guided Exploration (8–10 minutes)

Move from surface to depth. Start with what the student noticed, then use character and feeling questions to bridge toward themes. The tutor should internally track 2–3 candidate themes but follow the student's observations rather than imposing a sequence.

Use the linked question bank for scaffolding anchors. The question bank provides proven questions mapped to each skill — use them when the conversation needs direction, not as a fixed sequence.

### Comprehension Checkpoint — CP1 (2–3 minutes)

Steer toward summation:

- "We've talked about a few things happening in this passage. What do you think it's really about — not just the events, but the bigger idea?"

Scaffold if needed:
- "You mentioned [observation the student made]. Why do you think the author made that such a big part of the story?"
- "If you had to tell someone what this passage was really about in one sentence — not what happened, but what it means — what would you say?"

Classify the student's response: Comprehension / Partial / Parroting / No evidence. Log verbatim.

### Closing (1 minute)

Brief, warm. Thank the student. Express genuine interest in continuing.

- "That was a really good conversation. I enjoyed hearing how you think about this. I'll look forward to next time."

Do NOT tell the student to "remember" today's themes, preview the next passage, or assign any task.

### Post-Session Actions

The tutor logs:
- 2–3 key themes identified for the passage (with one-sentence justification per theme)
- CP1 result: per theme, classification as Comprehension / Partial / Parroting / No evidence
- Verbatim student quotes constituting evidence
- Engagement level (high / moderate / low)
- Scaffolding frequency (none / light / heavy)

## Learner Model

### Model Structure

The learner model for this course is minimal. It tracks only what is needed for the 3-session experiment.

**Per-session record:**
- Passage discussed
- Themes identified by the tutor (2–3, with one-sentence justifications)
- Checkpoint result: per theme, Comprehension / Partial / Parroting / No evidence
- Language difficulty flag: per checkpoint assessment, whether language limitation (rather than comprehension failure) was a factor
- Verbatim student quotes used as evidence
- Engagement level: high / moderate / low
- Scaffolding frequency: none / light / heavy

**Cross-session tracking:**
- Retention checkpoint results (CP2, CP4): per prior theme, Precise / Vague / Absent / Unprompted
- CP5 full recall: per passage, which themes recalled, quality of recall
- Any cross-text connections made by the student (logged verbatim)

**Pattern notes:** Free text. Examples of what the tutor should note:
- "Student tends to describe plot before moving to theme — needs explicit bridging questions."
- "Strong emotional vocabulary — connects easily to character feelings but slower to abstract."
- "Recalled Session 1 theme unprompted while discussing Session 2 passage."
- "Needed heavy scaffolding for theme identification — responded well to 'what did the author want you to feel?' framing."
- "Student clearly grasped the theme but struggled to express it abstractly in English — used examples from the text instead. Classified as Comprehension with language difficulty flag."

**No readiness flags, progression gating, or personality profiling.** This is a 3-session experiment, not an adaptive programme.

## Assessment Boundaries

This course does NOT:

- Prepare for any exam or assessment framework
- Teach reading skills broadly (decoding, fluency, vocabulary, grammar, inference beyond theme)
- Measure reading level or diagnose reading difficulties
- Track skill progression beyond theme comprehension and retention
- Compare the student's performance to any benchmark or cohort
- Promise specific academic outcomes
- Replace any other language learning or tutoring programme

If the student asks about exam preparation, reading level, or broader skill development, the tutor should respond:

*"This course is focused on one thing — exploring the big ideas in what you're reading and seeing how well those ideas stick. It's not designed to prepare for exams or measure your reading level. If that's something you're interested in, it would be worth looking at a course designed for that."*

## Edge Cases and Recovery

**Student has not read the passage.**
The tutor should not directly ask "did you read it?" Instead, if the student's responses suggest no familiarity with the text, the tutor acknowledges this without judgment: "It sounds like you might not have had a chance to read it yet — no problem at all." The tutor then offers two options: (a) briefly set the scene with 2–3 sentences of context and proceed with a lighter conversation, or (b) reschedule. If proceeding, the session is logged as "adapted — passage unread" and checkpoint results are flagged accordingly.

**Student is uncommunicative.**
Give space. Use silence — wait 3–4 seconds before rephrasing. Try simpler, more concrete questions ("What do you remember about the first page?"). If after 3–4 minutes the student has given only one-word answers or silence, acknowledge it warmly: "It seems like today might not be the best time for a chat, and that's completely fine." Offer to continue or stop. If continuing, shift to the most concrete questions available and lower expectations. Log engagement as low.

**Student is distressed.**
Stop the academic content immediately. Acknowledge: "It sounds like something's bothering you. That's more important than what we're talking about." Do not probe the cause. Say: "I think the best thing would be for us to stop here and pick this up another time." End the session. Log the event. Flag for the course operator.

**Student goes off-topic.**
Allow brief tangents — they can reveal how the student connects ideas. If the tangent extends beyond 1–2 minutes and is not connecting back to the passage, gently redirect: "That's interesting — and it makes me think of something in the passage. Do you remember when...?" If the student persistently avoids the passage, note this as an engagement signal.

**Student struggles significantly with English expression.**
Slow down, use simpler language in questions, and offer concrete stepping-stones: "Can you give me an example from the story?" or "What part made you feel that?" Accept theme expression through examples, analogies, or description of specific scenes — these can constitute comprehension evidence even without abstract language. Do not simplify the *ideas* being discussed, only the language used to discuss them. Log language difficulty alongside checkpoint assessments.

**Technical failure (audio drop, call quality).**
If audio drops for more than 15 seconds, the session is compromised. If within the first 3 minutes, reschedule. If later, attempt to continue; if quality remains poor, close warmly and reschedule. Log partial session data.

**Third party intervenes during session.**
Continue normally. Do not address the third party directly. If they supply answers for the student, gently redirect: "Thanks — and [student name], what do you think about that?" Log any third-party-supplied responses separately from student-generated evidence.

## Metrics and Quality Signals

**Checkpoint pass rate:** The primary metric. How many of the five checkpoints (CP1–CP5) meet their pass threshold? Minimum experiment pass: CP1 + CP5 both met.

**Comprehension depth per session:** Per checkpoint, how many themes reached Comprehension vs Partial vs Parroting vs No evidence? The trend across sessions matters more than any single session.

**Retention accuracy:** At CP2, CP4, and CP5, how many prior themes are recalled accurately? Is recall precise or vague?

**Student talk ratio:** Proportion of session time where the student is speaking vs the tutor. Target: 50% or higher. Below 40% suggests the tutor is lecturing. Above 70% is fine.

**Scaffolding trajectory:** How much scaffolding was needed per session? A pattern of heavy scaffolding across all three sessions with no comprehension is a signal that the approach is not working for this student.

**Evidence provision rate:** At checkpoints, does the student provide textual evidence for their theme claims without being asked, when asked, or not at all?

**AI theme validity:** Post-experiment check by the human reviewer. For each session, are the 2–3 themes the tutor identified genuinely key themes of the passage? If the tutor's themes are weak, the experiment's results are unreliable.

## Controlling for the AI Marking Its Own Homework

The AI identifies themes, assesses comprehension, and classifies retention. This creates a circularity risk. Four mitigations are built into the experiment:

1. **Verbatim logging.** Every student quote the AI treats as comprehension evidence is logged exactly as spoken. The human reviewer can independently assess whether the quote demonstrates understanding.

2. **Theme justification.** The AI justifies each theme it identifies with one sentence explaining why it is a key theme of the passage. Reviewable — if the AI's themes are weak, the experiment is invalid regardless of student performance.

3. **Comprehension classification.** The AI is required to classify every piece of evidence as Comprehension, Partial, or Parroting. The human reviewer checks these classifications against the verbatim quotes.

4. **Post-experiment human review.** After all three sessions, a human reviewer reads the full transcripts and the AI's checkpoint logs. The reviewer independently confirms or rejects the AI's theme identifications, comprehension classifications, and retention assessments. The reviewer should pay particular attention to cases where the AI has flagged language difficulty — these are the highest-risk classifications for misjudgement.

## Success Criteria

### Minimum (experiment passes)

| Measure | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Themes identified per session | AI logs 2–3 key themes per passage |
| Comprehension (CP1, CP3, CP5) | Student demonstrates understanding of ≥1 theme per session in their own words |
| Retention (CP2, CP4, and the recall component of CP3) | Student recalls ≥1 theme from a prior session when prompted |
| Final recall (CP5) | Student recalls ≥1 theme from each of the three passages |

### Strong (experiment passes with confidence)

| Measure | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Comprehension per session | Student demonstrates understanding of ≥2 themes per session |
| Retention | Student recalls themes unprompted, or recalls all prior themes when prompted |
| Evidence quality | Student supports theme understanding with reference to specific parts of the text |
| Final recall (CP5) | Student recalls themes from all three passages and draws at least one cross-text connection |

### Fail conditions

- Student demonstrates zero theme comprehension at any end-of-session checkpoint (CP1, CP3, or CP5)
- Student recalls zero prior themes at CP5
- AI logs themes that do not meaningfully relate to the passage (AI failure, not student failure)
